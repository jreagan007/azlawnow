## ARS § 12-542 — Personal Injury Statute of Limitations

**Legal principle**: An action for personal injury must be commenced within **two years** after the cause of action accrues.

**Exact statutory text (key clause)**: "There shall be commenced and prosecuted within two years after the cause of action accrues, and not afterward, the following actions: 1. For injuries done to the person of another including causes of action for medical malpractice as defined in section 12-561."

**Accrual rule**: The SOL begins on the date the injury occurs. For latent injuries (those not immediately apparent), the discovery rule delays accrual until the injured person knows or should know of the injury and its causal connection.

**Scope**: Personal injury. Applies to car crashes, truck crashes, motorcycle crashes, pedestrian crashes, dog bites, premises liability, slip-and-fall, rideshare crashes, and other private-party tort claims. Does NOT govern public-entity defendants (see ARS § 12-821 and § 12-821.01 below).

**azleg.gov URL**: `https://www.azleg.gov/ars/12/00542.htm`

**Common misquote patterns** (fabrication class hits):
- "three years" — WRONG; ARS 12-542 is two years
- "one year" — WRONG for PI (one year applies to libel/slander under 12-541)
- "two years from the date of the accident report" — WRONG; accrual runs from the injury, not the report
- Citing 12-542 for wrongful death — WRONG; wrongful death is governed by 12-611/612

## ARS § 12-611 — Wrongful Death Action (Right of Action)

**Legal principle**: When a person's death is caused by the wrongful act, neglect, or default of another, a civil action for damages may be brought against the person or entity responsible.

**Exact statutory text (key clause)**: "When death of a person is caused by wrongful act, neglect or default, and the act, neglect or default is such as would, if death had not ensued, have entitled the party injured to maintain an action to recover damages in respect thereof, then, and in every such case, the person who or the corporation which would have been liable if death had not ensued shall be liable to an action for damages..."

**SOL**: Two years from the date of death (same as ARS § 12-542 for PI; the wrongful death cause of action accrues on the date of death, not the date of the underlying negligent act).

**azleg.gov URL**: `https://www.azleg.gov/ars/12/00611.htm`

**Common misquote patterns**:
- "three years for wrongful death" — WRONG; it's two years
- Citing 12-542 alone for wrongful death — WRONG; the proper citation is 12-611 (right of action) + 12-542 (the limitation period)
- Omitting that the decedent must have had a viable PI claim if they had survived

## ARS § 12-612 — Wrongful Death Survivors (Who May Sue)

**Legal principle**: Defines who is entitled to bring a wrongful death action in Arizona and how damages are allocated.

**Eligible survivors** (in order):
1. Surviving spouse
2. Child or children of the deceased
3. Parent or parents of the deceased (if no surviving spouse or children)
4. Surviving brothers or sisters (if no surviving spouse, children, or parents)
5. If no surviving relatives under the above categories: the estate's personal representative

**Damages**: Distributed for the benefit of the surviving parties listed above. Each eligible survivor recovers for their own loss (loss of companionship, financial support, consortium). The personal representative files on behalf of survivors.

**azleg.gov URL**: `https://www.azleg.gov/ars/12/00612.htm`

**Common misquote patterns**:
- "Only a spouse can file a wrongful death claim" — WRONG; children, parents, and siblings may also file depending on who survives
- "The estate automatically collects damages" — WRONG; damages go to eligible survivors, not the estate generally
- Omitting that parents can file when no spouse or children survive

## ARS § 12-821 — Public Entity Suit Limitation (1-Year Suit Window)

**Legal principle**: Actions against a public entity or public employee must be filed within **one year** after the cause of action accrues. This is a shorter suit window than the general 2-year PI SOL under ARS § 12-542.

**Exact statutory text (key clause)**: "All actions against any public entity or public employee shall be brought within one year after the cause of action accrues and not afterward."

**Relationship to 12-821.01**: The 180-day notice requirement under § 12-821.01 is a CONDITION PRECEDENT to filing suit. If the notice is timely filed, the injured person then has 1 year (under § 12-821) from the date the cause of action accrued to file the actual lawsuit, subject to tolling during the 60-day claims-review period.

**azleg.gov URL**: `https://www.azleg.gov/ars/12/00821.htm`

**Common misquote patterns**:
- Applying the 2-year PI SOL (12-542) to public-entity defendants — WRONG; 12-821 is the controlling statute for public-entity suits
- Confusing the 1-year suit window with the 180-day notice window

## ARS § 12-821.01 — Notice of Claim Against Public Entity (180-Day Notice)

**Legal principle**: Before suing a public entity or public employee in Arizona, an injured person must file a formal "notice of claim" within **180 days** after the cause of action accrues. Failure to file a timely notice is a COMPLETE BAR to the lawsuit — it is jurisdictional, not a mere procedural defect.

**Exact statutory text (key clause)**: "Persons who have claims against a public entity or a public employee shall file claims with the person or persons authorized to accept service for the public entity or public employee as set forth in the Arizona rules of civil procedure within one hundred eighty days after the cause of action accrues."

**What the notice must contain**: The notice must contain enough information to allow the public entity to investigate and evaluate the claim: identity of the claimant, date/time/location of incident, nature of the claim, and the amount demanded (or a statement that the amount will be determined).

**Who is covered**: Any Arizona public entity — ADOT, cities, towns, counties, school districts, community college districts, state agencies, state universities (ASU, UA, NAU), and their employees acting within the scope of their employment.

**azleg.gov URL**: `https://www.azleg.gov/ars/12/00821-01.htm`

**CRITICAL distinction**: The 180-day notice is a BAR, not a defect. Courts have repeatedly held that no excuse (attorney mistake, ignorance, medical incapacitation unless extreme circumstances) will excuse failure to timely file. Every article touching public-entity defendants must state this clearly.

**Common misquote patterns** (CRITICAL fabrication class hits):
- "You have 180 days to sue a city" — WRONG; 180 days is for notice, not the lawsuit
- "File within two years even against the city" — WRONG; notice must precede suit by 180 days
- Omitting the notice requirement entirely — the most dangerous omission
- "It's just a formality" — WRONG; failure to file notice is a complete bar with very narrow exceptions

## ARS § 12-2505 — Pure Comparative Fault

**Legal principle**: Arizona follows PURE comparative fault. A plaintiff's recovery is reduced by their percentage of fault, but there is NO threshold percentage that bars recovery entirely. A plaintiff found 99% at fault may still recover 1% of their damages.

**Exact statutory text (key clause)**: "The defense of contributory negligence or of assumption of risk is in all cases whatsoever a question of fact and shall at all times be left to the jury. If the jury finds that the claimant was also at fault, the claimant's damages shall be reduced in proportion to their contributory fault."

**azleg.gov URL**: `https://www.azleg.gov/ars/12/02505.htm`

**Common misquote patterns** (HIGH fabrication class hits):
- "If you're more than 50% at fault you can't recover" — WRONG; that is MODIFIED comparative fault (used in other states)
- "Contributory negligence bars your claim" — WRONG; that is CONTRIBUTORY negligence (used in very few states, not AZ)
- "Arizona limits recovery if you share fault" — misleading; recovery is reduced but not barred
- Omitting "pure" when describing AZ's comparative fault system

## ARS § 12-2506 — Several Liability Only (No Joint-and-Several for Most Torts)

**Legal principle**: In Arizona, each defendant in a multi-defendant tort case is liable only for their proportionate share of the plaintiff's damages — the share corresponding to their percentage of fault. There is no joint-and-several liability for most torts (meaning no one defendant can be held liable for another defendant's share).

**Exceptions**: Joint-and-several liability remains available for (1) intentional torts, (2) torts where the defendant acted in concert with another tortfeasor, and (3) certain environmental torts.

**azleg.gov URL**: `https://www.azleg.gov/ars/12/02506.htm`

**Common misquote patterns**:
- "All defendants are jointly liable for the full amount" — WRONG for most AZ torts
- Failing to note the intentional-tort exception when discussing assault, battery, or intentional injury cases
- Confusing with pre-1987 joint-and-several rules

## ARS § 28-4009 — Minimum Motor Vehicle Liability Limits

**Legal principle**: Arizona law requires minimum motor vehicle liability insurance of:
- $25,000 per person for bodily injury
- $50,000 per accident for bodily injury (total for all injured persons)
- $15,000 per accident for property damage

Commonly expressed as **25/50/15**.

**azleg.gov URL**: `https://www.azleg.gov/ars/28/04009.htm`

**Common misquote patterns**:
- "Arizona requires $100,000 minimum" — WRONG; minimums are 25/50/15
- "$25,000 per accident" — WRONG; $25,000 is per person; $50,000 is per accident
- Treating minimums as "sufficient" coverage for serious injury — misleading; minimums are rarely adequate

## ARS § 39-121 — Public Records (Access to Government Records)

**Legal principle**: All public records and other matters in the custody of any officer shall be open to inspection by any person at all times during office hours. The law's "promptly" standard has been interpreted by the AG as 10 business days for a response.

**Scope**: Applies to all state, county, and municipal agencies — including ADOT crash reports, inspection records, daycare violation reports (DHS), school incident records (ADE), nursing home inspection findings (DHS), and law enforcement reports.

**Journalism fee waiver**: Requesters acting as journalists or in the public interest may request waiver of copying or access fees. The agency has discretion but must consider whether disclosure serves the public interest.

**azleg.gov URL**: `https://www.azleg.gov/ars/39/00121.htm`

**AG guidance URL**: `https://www.azag.gov/open-government`

**Common misquote patterns**:
- "The agency has 10 days to respond by law" — MISLEADING; "promptly" is the statutory standard; 10 days is AG interpretation, not hard statutory text
- "Fee waivers are automatic for journalists" — WRONG; fee waivers are discretionary
- "ADOT crash reports are not public records" — WRONG; crash data is public record under 39-121

## AZ Constitution, Article II, Section 31 — No Damages Cap

**Legal principle**: "No law shall be enacted in this state limiting the amount of damages to be recovered for causing the death or injury of any person."

**Effect**: Arizona is one of a small number of states with a constitutional prohibition on damages caps. This means:
- No statutory cap on general damages (pain and suffering, loss of consortium, emotional distress)
- No cap on wrongful death damages
- No cap on punitive damages by statute (though courts may review excessive punitive awards under due-process standards)

**Source URL**: `https://www.azleg.gov/constitution/` → Article II, Section 31

**CRITICAL guard**: The inverse error is equally dangerous. An article must not suggest that AZ damages are capped. Any language like "your damages may be limited by Arizona law" is a fabrication class hit unless the context refers to a specific contractual limitation or a specific category where limitation is permitted (e.g., government sovereign immunity context under § 12-821 where the amount-demanded requirement may interact with the claim).

**Common misquote patterns** (CRITICAL fabrication class hits):
- "Arizona caps pain and suffering at $X" — WRONG; unconstitutional
- "Punitive damages are capped at 3x compensatory in Arizona" — WRONG; no statutory cap exists (some case law guidance on excessiveness, but no statutory dollar cap)
- Confusing AZ with other states that have caps (California MICRA, Texas § 74.301, etc.)

## Arizona Rule of Evidence 803(6) — Business Records Exception

**Legal principle**: A record of an act, event, condition, opinion, or diagnosis is not excluded by the rule against hearsay if:
- The record was made at or near the time of the act, event, condition, or diagnosis
- By, or from information transmitted by, a person with knowledge
- The record was kept in the course of a regularly conducted activity of a business
- Making the record was a regular practice of that activity

**Relevance to AZ PI practice**: ADOT crash reports, trucking company driver logs and inspection records, nursing home incident reports, daycare staff records, school disciplinary files, and hospital admission records are commonly authenticated under this exception to be introduced as evidence.

**Source URL**: `https://www.azcourts.gov/rulesofcourt/` → Arizona Rules of Evidence → Rule 803

**Common misquote patterns**:
- "ADOT crash reports are automatically admissible" — OVERSIMPLIFICATION; they still need proper foundation and authentication under 803(6)
- "Any business record comes in under the hearsay exception" — WRONG; the foundation requirements must be met
