Pedestrian crashes in Arizona produce some of the most severe injuries in personal injury law. A person on foot has zero structural protection against a vehicle moving at 40 or 50 mph. The physics are brutal and the legal questions that follow are often misunderstood.

Arizona's pedestrian statutes are spread across several sections of Title 28. They establish when pedestrians have the right of way, when they don't, and what duties drivers owe regardless of who had priority. This guide covers each statute, how comparative negligence applies, and what legal options exist when a pedestrian is struck by a vehicle.

## Right of Way in Crosswalks: ARS 28-792

ARS 28-792 is the foundational pedestrian right-of-way statute in Arizona. It establishes that a driver must yield the right of way to a pedestrian crossing the roadway within a crosswalk when the pedestrian is in the driver's half of the roadway, or when the pedestrian is approaching closely enough from the opposite half to be in danger.

This applies to both marked and unmarked crosswalks. That distinction matters. An *unmarked crosswalk* exists at every intersection by default under Arizona law. It's the natural extension of the sidewalk or shoulder across the roadway. Pedestrians don't need painted lines to have the right of way at an intersection. If there's an intersection, there's a crosswalk, whether the city has painted one or not.

The statute also prohibits pedestrians from suddenly leaving the curb and walking or running into the path of a vehicle that's close enough to constitute an immediate hazard. This is the balance the legislature built into the law. Pedestrians have priority, but they can't materialize in front of a car and claim right of way.

<Callout type="info" title="Marked vs. Unmarked Crosswalks">
  Many people believe a crosswalk only exists where lines are painted on the road. That's incorrect under Arizona law. Every intersection has a legal crosswalk at the extension of the sidewalk or roadway shoulder, whether it's marked or not. Drivers must yield to pedestrians in both.
</Callout>

There's a practical issue here. Drivers rarely know that unmarked crosswalks exist. They see an intersection with no painted crosswalk and assume pedestrians have no right of way. That assumption is wrong, but it contributes to a significant number of crashes where drivers fail to yield at uncontrolled intersections.

## Pedestrian Duties Outside Crosswalks: ARS 28-793

ARS 28-793 flips the priority. When a pedestrian crosses a roadway at any point other than within a marked or unmarked crosswalk, the pedestrian must yield the right of way to all vehicles on the roadway.

This is what most people mean when they say "jaywalking," though that term doesn't appear in the statute. The pedestrian doesn't lose all protection. ARS 28-796 still imposes a duty on drivers to exercise care. But the pedestrian loses right-of-way priority.

From a liability standpoint, crossing outside a crosswalk creates a question of comparative fault. It doesn't bar recovery. Arizona abolished contributory negligence decades ago. A pedestrian crossing mid-block can still recover damages if the driver was speeding, distracted, impaired, or otherwise negligent. The pedestrian's recovery gets reduced by their share of fault.

## Between Adjacent Signalized Intersections: ARS 28-794

ARS 28-794 adds a more specific rule for urban corridors. When a pedestrian is between two adjacent intersections that both have traffic signals, the pedestrian must use the crosswalk at one of those signalized intersections to cross.

This statute applies in areas with closely spaced signals, which is common in downtown corridors and commercial districts. The intent is straightforward. If two signalized crossings bracket a block, the pedestrian should use one of them rather than crossing mid-block.

Violating ARS 28-794 doesn't eliminate a pedestrian's right to recover. But it does create a stronger comparative fault argument for the defense. An insurance company will point to this statute and argue the pedestrian had a safe, signalized crossing available within a short distance and chose not to use it.

In practice, the strength of this argument depends on the specific facts. How far apart were the signals? Was the mid-block crossing point obscured from driver view? Was the driver exceeding the speed limit? Every detail matters.

## The Driver's Duty of Care: ARS 28-796

This is the statute pedestrian injury attorneys rely on most, and it's the one insurance adjusters try hardest to minimize.

ARS 28-796 requires every driver to exercise due care to avoid colliding with a pedestrian on any roadway. It further requires drivers to give warning by sounding a horn when necessary and to exercise proper precaution upon observing any child or any obviously confused or incapacitated person on a roadway.

The critical phrase is "on any roadway." Not "in a crosswalk." Not "at an intersection." Any roadway. This means a driver's duty to exercise care applies even when the pedestrian is outside a crosswalk, crossing mid-block, or otherwise not in a position of legal priority.

<Callout type="important" title="ARS 28-796 Applies Everywhere">
  A driver's duty to exercise due care exists regardless of whether the pedestrian has the right of way. This statute applies on every roadway in Arizona. A driver who strikes a pedestrian outside a crosswalk can still be found negligent under ARS 28-796 if they failed to exercise reasonable care.
</Callout>

In crash reconstruction, this statute creates a two-part analysis. First, did the pedestrian have the right of way? Second, regardless of that answer, did the driver exercise due care? A "no" on the second question creates driver liability even when the answer to the first question was also "no" for the pedestrian.

## Comparative Negligence: ARS 12-2505

Arizona follows a pure comparative negligence system. ARS 12-2505 states that the defense of contributory negligence doesn't bar recovery in a tort action. Instead, the finder of fact assigns a percentage of fault to each party, and the plaintiff's recovery is reduced by their share.

For pedestrian crashes, this means a pedestrian found 40% at fault for crossing outside a crosswalk can still recover 60% of their damages. A pedestrian found 80% at fault can still recover 20%. There's no threshold that bars recovery entirely.

  label="Arizona's comparative negligence system allows recovery at any fault percentage"

This is one of the most plaintiff-favorable comparative negligence systems in the country. Some states use a modified system that bars recovery once the plaintiff's fault exceeds 50% or 51%. Arizona has no such cutoff.

In practice, the comparative fault percentages in pedestrian cases are heavily litigated. The insurance company will argue the pedestrian was primarily at fault. The pedestrian's attorney will point to the driver's speed, distraction, failure to yield, visibility, and the road design itself. Both arguments center on the specific facts of the crash.

Factors that commonly increase the driver's share of fault include exceeding the posted speed limit, texting or using a phone at the time of the crash, failing to activate headlights in low-light conditions, ignoring yield signs or signal indications, and failing to check crosswalks during right turns.

Factors that commonly increase the pedestrian's share include crossing outside a crosswalk when a signalized crossing was nearby, wearing dark clothing at night without reflective material, entering the roadway from between parked vehicles, and walking while impaired by alcohol or drugs.

## Hit-and-Run: ARS 28-661 Through 28-665

When a driver strikes a pedestrian and leaves the scene, the criminal and civil consequences escalate sharply.

ARS 28-661 requires any driver involved in a crash resulting in injury or death to immediately stop at the scene. The driver must provide name, address, vehicle registration, and driver's license information to the injured person or to law enforcement. The driver must also render reasonable assistance, including calling emergency medical services.

ARS 28-662 covers crashes involving death or serious physical injury. Leaving the scene of a crash that results in death is a class 3 felony. Leaving the scene of a crash that results in serious physical injury is a class 3 felony as well. These carry potential prison sentences of two to eight years for a first offense.

ARS 28-663 addresses crashes involving injury that isn't classified as serious physical injury. This is a class 5 felony, carrying a potential sentence of six months to two and a half years.

<Callout type="warning" title="Hit-and-Run Is a Felony">
  Leaving the scene of a pedestrian crash that causes injury is a felony in Arizona. If the pedestrian suffers serious physical injury or death, the driver faces a class 3 felony with mandatory prison time. There is no scenario where fleeing helps the driver's legal situation.
</Callout>

On the civil side, a hit-and-run makes the driver's defense dramatically harder. Juries don't look favorably on someone who struck a pedestrian and drove away. The act of fleeing is admissible as evidence of consciousness of guilt.

For the injured pedestrian, a hit-and-run creates a practical problem: identifying the driver. When the driver can't be identified, the pedestrian's own uninsured motorist coverage becomes the primary source of compensation.

## Uninsured and Underinsured Motorist Coverage for Pedestrians

Many people don't realize that their own auto insurance policy can cover them as pedestrians. Arizona requires auto insurers to offer uninsured motorist (UM) and underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage. If a policyholder is struck by a car while walking, their UM/UIM coverage can apply.

This matters in two common scenarios. First, hit-and-run crashes where the driver is never identified. The fleeing driver is treated as an uninsured motorist, and the pedestrian's own UM policy provides compensation. Second, crashes where the driver's liability insurance is insufficient to cover the pedestrian's damages. The pedestrian's UIM policy covers the gap up to the UIM policy limits.

Arizona's minimum liability coverage is $25,000 per person. Pedestrian injuries routinely produce medical bills that exceed this amount by tens of thousands of dollars. A traumatic brain injury or spinal cord injury can generate medical costs exceeding $500,000 in the first year alone. Without adequate UIM coverage, the pedestrian may face a significant uncompensated loss.

This is why personal injury attorneys in Arizona consistently advise carrying UM/UIM limits that match your liability limits. It's coverage that protects you when someone else's insurance isn't enough.

## Government Liability for Dangerous Road Design

Can you sue a city or county for designing a road that's dangerous for pedestrians? In Arizona, the answer is conditional. Government entities have qualified immunity under ARS 12-820 and related statutes, but that immunity doesn't cover every situation.

ARS 12-820.03 provides that a public entity isn't liable for injuries caused by a plan or design for construction or improvement to roads if the plan or design was approved in advance by the governing body or an employee exercising discretionary authority. This is the *plan or design immunity* defense, and it's powerful.

However, there are exceptions. If a government entity knew about a dangerous condition and failed to warn or repair it within a reasonable time, the immunity may not apply. If a road was modified after the original design in a way that created the hazard, plan or design immunity may not protect the modification. If the road didn't conform to the approved design, immunity may not apply.

ARS 12-820.01 addresses the failure to maintain. If a dangerous condition existed because the government failed to maintain the road, and the government had notice of the condition, liability can attach.

In practice, suing a government entity for dangerous road design in Arizona is difficult but not impossible. The claim typically requires evidence that the government had notice of a dangerous condition, that the condition was the result of a maintenance failure rather than an original design choice, and that the government failed to act within a reasonable time.

Filing requirements are strict. ARS 12-821.01 requires a notice of claim to be filed with the government entity within 180 days of the injury. Missing this deadline can bar the claim entirely, even if the statute of limitations hasn't expired.

<Callout type="warning" title="180-Day Notice Requirement">
  Claims against Arizona cities, counties, or state agencies require a written notice of claim filed within 180 days of the date of injury. This deadline is shorter than the two-year statute of limitations and applies separately. Missing the 180-day notice deadline can permanently bar the claim.
</Callout>

## Statute of Limitations: ARS 12-542

ARS 12-542 sets the statute of limitations for personal injury claims in Arizona at two years from the date of the injury. For pedestrian crashes, the clock starts on the date of the crash.

Two years sounds like a long time. It isn't. Building a pedestrian crash case requires obtaining the police report, collecting medical records from every treating provider, documenting the crash scene (which can change as roads are repaired or modified), identifying witnesses, and potentially retaining a crash reconstruction expert.

If the claim involves a government entity, the 180-day notice of claim deadline under ARS 12-821.01 creates an even shorter timeline. That's six months. If the pedestrian is seriously injured and hospitalized, six months can pass before they're even able to meet with an attorney.

There are limited exceptions. For minors, the statute of limitations is tolled until the child turns 18, at which point the two-year clock begins. For individuals who are legally incapacitated, tolling may apply during the period of incapacity. But these exceptions are narrow.

The practical advice is straightforward. If you've been hit by a car as a pedestrian, contact an attorney as soon as you're physically able. The earlier the investigation begins, the better the evidence preservation and the stronger the case.

## Wrongful Death in Pedestrian Crashes

When a pedestrian crash results in death, the claim shifts from personal injury to wrongful death under ARS 12-611 through 12-613. Arizona's wrongful death statute permits the surviving spouse, children, parents, or personal representative of the estate to bring a claim.

Damages in a wrongful death case include the decedent's lost earnings and earning capacity, loss of companionship and consortium, funeral and burial expenses, and the pain and suffering the decedent experienced between the crash and death.

Arizona doesn't cap non-economic damages in wrongful death cases. The Arizona Constitution, Article 2, Section 31, prohibits the legislature from placing a cap on the amount recoverable as damages for death or injury. This is significant. Many states cap non-economic damages. Arizona doesn't.

The two-year statute of limitations applies to wrongful death claims as well, running from the date of death. If the pedestrian survived for a period after the crash before dying from their injuries, the limitations period may differ from the date of the crash itself.

## Building a Pedestrian Crash Case

Pedestrian crash cases require specific evidence beyond what a typical car crash case demands. The investigation should address whether a crosswalk existed (marked or unmarked), the signal state at the time of the crash, the speed of the vehicle, lighting conditions, the driver's line of sight, whether the pedestrian was visible, and the road design itself.

Road design evidence can be critical. If the speed limit was 45 mph in a commercial area with regular pedestrian traffic, that's a factor. If the nearest crosswalk was a quarter mile away, that's a factor. If the sidewalk ended and forced the pedestrian into the road, that's a factor. If the lighting was inadequate, that's a factor.
