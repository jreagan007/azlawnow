You were walking. A car hit you. Everything after that moment is confusing, painful, and overwhelming. This guide walks you through what to do right now, step by step, so you don't have to figure it out alone.

The decisions you make in the hours and days after a pedestrian crash affect your health, your financial recovery, and your legal options. Some of these steps matter more than you'd think. Missing even one can create problems months down the road.

## At the Scene

If you're conscious and able to act, the first minutes after the crash are critical. Here's what to do and what to avoid.

**Don't move if you suspect a head, neck, or back injury.** Pedestrian crashes often involve head impact with the vehicle hood or windshield, followed by impact with the pavement. Moving with an undiagnosed spinal injury can cause permanent damage. Stay still and wait for paramedics.

**Call 911.** Even if your injuries seem minor. Even if the driver says they'll pay for everything. Even if you feel embarrassed. The 911 call creates an official record. The responding officers generate a police report. That report is the foundation of your injury claim.

**Get the driver's information.** If you're physically able, write down or photograph the driver's license plate number, their name, phone number, driver's license number, and insurance company. If the driver hands you a card, photograph it. Don't rely on memory. Adrenaline distorts recall.

**Look for witnesses.** Anyone who saw what happened. Other pedestrians, people in nearby cars, employees at nearby businesses. Get their names and phone numbers. Witnesses disappear fast. After a week, they're nearly impossible to find.

**Don't apologize or admit fault.** This is hard when you're in shock. Your instinct might be to say "I'm sorry" or "I shouldn't have been crossing there." Don't. Anything you say at the scene can be used against you later. Stick to the facts: what happened, where it hurts, and that you need medical help.

<Callout type="important" title="Protect Your Rights at the Scene">
  Don't sign anything the driver or their insurance company offers you at the scene. Don't agree to settle on the spot. Don't give a recorded statement to anyone other than law enforcement. These early interactions can limit your ability to recover full compensation.
</Callout>

## Getting Medical Care

Pedestrian injuries are almost always more severe than the injuries car occupants sustain in the same crash. There's a simple reason for this. You had no seatbelt, no airbag, no steel frame around you. Your body absorbed the full force of the impact.

**Go to the emergency room.** Not urgent care. Not your primary care doctor next week. The ER. Pedestrian crashes frequently cause internal injuries that don't produce immediate symptoms. A ruptured spleen might not cause pain for hours. A brain bleed can develop gradually. Fractured ribs might feel like bruising at first.

The ER visit does two things. It gets you the imaging and evaluation you need to catch injuries early. And it creates a medical record that links your injuries directly to the crash. Insurance companies look for gaps between the crash date and the first medical visit. The longer the gap, the harder they argue that your injuries aren't from the crash.

**Follow up with every recommended specialist.** If the ER refers you to an orthopedist, a neurologist, or a concussion specialist, go. Every missed appointment becomes ammunition for the insurance company. They'll argue that if you were really hurt, you'd have kept the appointment.

**Keep every medical document.** Every bill, every receipt, every discharge summary, every imaging report. Keep physical copies and digital copies. Create a folder. This sounds tedious. It matters enormously when calculating your damages six or 12 months later.

## What to Document

Evidence from the crash scene has a shelf life. Road conditions change. Traffic signals get retimed. Surveillance footage gets overwritten, usually within 30 to 72 hours. The faster you document, the more evidence you preserve.

**Photos to take at the scene.** The intersection or location where you were hit. Whether there was a crosswalk (or wasn't one). The state of the traffic signals. The vehicle that hit you, including damage to the hood, windshield, and bumper. Your injuries. Your clothing. Skid marks or debris on the road. Any surveillance cameras you can see on buildings, traffic poles, or nearby businesses.

**Photos to take later.** Your injuries as they develop. Bruising often looks worse at 48 to 72 hours than it did at the scene. Photograph your injuries every day for the first two weeks. Save every image with a date stamp.

**The police report.** This won't be available at the scene. You'll need to request it from the responding agency, usually the city police department or the Maricopa County Sheriff's Office. The report number should be on the card the officer gives you. Request the report within a week. It contains the officer's narrative, a diagram of the crash, driver and witness information, and any citations issued.

**Your own written account.** As soon as you're able, write down everything you remember about the crash. What direction were you walking. What you saw before the impact. Whether you were in a crosswalk. What the driver did after hitting you. Write it the same day if possible. Memory degrades fast, especially after a traumatic event.

### Hit and Run: What to Do When the Driver Flees

This is one of the most frustrating and frightening situations. You're hurt, the driver is gone, and you might feel like there's nothing you can do. That's not true.

**Call 911 immediately.** Report the hit-and-run. Provide whatever details you can about the vehicle: color, make, model, direction of travel, any portion of the license plate you remember.

**Look for cameras.** Traffic cameras, red-light cameras, business security cameras, doorbell cameras on nearby homes. Point these out to the responding officers. This footage is the most common way hit-and-run drivers get identified.

**Talk to witnesses.** Someone else may have seen the vehicle or caught the plate number. Bystanders sometimes take photos or video on their phones without the injured person knowing.

**File a claim under your own auto insurance.** If you carry uninsured motorist (UM) coverage, it applies to hit-and-run crashes where the driver isn't identified. Your own policy becomes your primary source of compensation.

<Callout type="warning" title="Surveillance Footage Disappears Fast">
  Most business security cameras overwrite footage every 48 to 72 hours. If there were cameras near the crash scene, time is critical. Tell the responding officers and your attorney immediately. We can send a preservation letter to the business within 24 hours.
</Callout>

## Dealing With Insurance

The insurance process after a pedestrian crash is more complicated than a typical car-on-car collision. Multiple insurance policies may apply, and the order of priority depends on who caused the crash and what coverage you carry.

**The driver's liability insurance pays first.** If the driver was at fault, their auto liability policy covers your medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, and other damages up to their policy limits. Arizona's minimum liability coverage is $25,000 per person. Many drivers carry only the minimum. Pedestrian injuries often exceed $25,000 by a wide margin.

**Your own uninsured/underinsured motorist (UM/UIM) coverage fills the gap.** If the driver's policy isn't enough, or if the driver was uninsured, your own UM/UIM coverage applies. This is coverage you pay for on your own auto policy. It protects you when you're injured as a pedestrian, not just when you're in your car.

**MedPay coverage on your auto policy.** If you carry Medical Payments coverage, it can pay your medical bills regardless of who was at fault. MedPay typically ranges from $1,000 to $10,000. It pays quickly and doesn't require proving the other driver was at fault.

**Your health insurance.** Your health insurance covers your medical treatment like any other injury. But if you later recover a settlement from the at-fault driver's insurance, your health insurer may have a right to reimbursement for what they paid. This is called *subrogation*. Your attorney handles the subrogation negotiation to minimize what you owe back.

**Don't give a recorded statement to the driver's insurance company without legal advice.** The driver's insurer will contact you quickly. They'll sound friendly and concerned. They'll ask you to describe what happened "in your own words." What they're doing is looking for statements they can use to reduce your claim. You have no obligation to give them a recorded statement. Politely decline and tell them your attorney will be in touch.

## When You Weren't in a Crosswalk

This is one of the biggest misconceptions families have after a pedestrian crash. They think that because they were crossing the street outside a crosswalk, they can't recover anything. That's wrong.

Arizona uses a *comparative negligence* system. That means fault gets split between the parties based on the evidence. Even if you were 40% at fault for crossing mid-block, you can still recover 60% of your damages. There's no threshold in Arizona that bars your claim entirely.

And here's the part that matters most: even when you're crossing outside a crosswalk, the driver still has a legal duty to exercise care to avoid hitting you. That's ARS 28-796. If the driver was speeding, texting, running a red light, or simply not paying attention, they share fault. In many cases, they carry most of it.

The facts that strengthen your case even without a crosswalk include the driver exceeding the speed limit, the driver using a phone at the time of the crash, poor lighting or missing street lights, no nearby crosswalk available (forcing you to cross mid-block), wide roads with no pedestrian refuge, and evidence that drivers regularly exceed the posted speed in that area.

Don't assume you have no case. Let an attorney review the facts before you make that decision.

## Common Pedestrian Injuries

The human body isn't designed to absorb the impact of a vehicle moving at 30, 40, or 50 mph. Pedestrian crash injuries tend to be catastrophic. Understanding what you might be dealing with helps you make better decisions about treatment and legal timelines.

**Traumatic brain injury (TBI).** The most common severe injury in pedestrian crashes. The initial impact throws the pedestrian onto the vehicle, and the secondary impact drops them onto pavement. Both impacts can cause brain injury. Symptoms range from concussion (headaches, confusion, memory problems) to severe TBI with long-term cognitive impairment. Some TBI symptoms don't appear for days or weeks.

**Spinal cord injuries.** The force of a vehicle striking a person on foot can fracture vertebrae and damage the spinal cord. Depending on the location and severity, spinal cord injuries can cause partial or complete paralysis. Recovery timelines range from months to permanent disability.

**Broken pelvis and legs.** The "bumper hit" pattern is characteristic of pedestrian crashes. The vehicle's bumper strikes the pedestrian at hip or thigh height, fracturing the pelvis, femur, tibia, or fibula. Pelvic fractures are especially dangerous because they can cause internal bleeding. Leg fractures often require surgery, hardware (plates, screws, rods), and months of physical therapy.

**Internal organ damage.** Impact with the vehicle or the ground can rupture the spleen, lacerate the liver, or cause internal bleeding that isn't immediately obvious. This is why the ER visit isn't optional. Internal injuries that go undiagnosed for hours can become life-threatening.

**Soft tissue and degloving injuries.** Road rash, deep lacerations, and degloving injuries (where skin is torn from underlying tissue) are common when a pedestrian slides across pavement. These injuries often require multiple surgeries and can result in permanent scarring.

<Callout type="info" title="Recovery Timelines Vary Widely">
  A fractured leg from a pedestrian crash might heal in three to six months. A traumatic brain injury might affect you for years. A spinal cord injury might be permanent. Don't settle your case until you understand the full scope of your injuries and your expected recovery. Settling too early almost always means settling for less than you deserve.
</Callout>

**Recovery timelines by injury type.** These are general ranges. Every case is different.

| Injury | Typical Recovery | Long-Term Impact |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Concussion / mild TBI | 2 to 12 weeks | Memory and focus issues may persist |
| Moderate to severe TBI | 6 months to years | Cognitive impairment, personality changes |
| Broken leg (tibia/fibula) | 3 to 6 months | Chronic pain, arthritis in some cases |
| Pelvic fracture | 3 to 12 months | Mobility limitations, chronic pain |
| Spinal cord injury | Months to permanent | Partial or complete paralysis |
| Internal organ damage | 2 to 8 weeks (surgical) | Depends on organ and severity |

## Your Legal Options

You have the right to pursue compensation for your injuries, lost income, pain, and the ways this crash has changed your life. Here's what the legal process looks like.

**The two-year deadline.** Arizona law gives you two years from the date of the crash to file a personal injury lawsuit (ARS 12-542). If your claim involves a city, county, or state agency (for example, a dangerous road design claim), you must file a notice of claim within 180 days. These deadlines aren't flexible.

**What we investigate.** When you call us, we start building your case immediately. We pull the police report. We visit the crash scene. We photograph the intersection, crosswalk markings, traffic signals, lighting, and sidewalk conditions. We request surveillance footage from nearby businesses. We pull ADOT crash history for the intersection to show whether it has a pattern of pedestrian crashes. We subpoena the driver's phone records to check for distracted driving.

**What damages you can recover.** Arizona law allows compensation for medical expenses (past and future), lost wages and reduced earning capacity, pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life, permanent disability or disfigurement, and in wrongful death cases, funeral expenses and loss of companionship for the family.

## FAQ

<FAQ>
  <Question q="What should I do first after being hit by a car as a pedestrian?">
    Don't move if you suspect a head, neck, or back injury. Call 911 immediately. A police report is essential evidence. If you're able, get the driver's information and photograph the scene, the vehicle, and any traffic signals or crosswalk markings. Then go to the emergency room, even if you think your injuries are minor.
  </Question>

  <Question q="Can I recover compensation if I wasn't in a crosswalk?">
    Yes. Arizona's comparative negligence law (ARS 12-2505) allows you to recover damages even if you were partially at fault. The driver still has a legal duty to exercise care to avoid hitting pedestrians, regardless of whether you were in a crosswalk. Your recovery is reduced by your share of fault but isn't eliminated.
  </Question>

  <Question q="What if the driver who hit me drove away?">
    Call 911 and report the hit-and-run. Provide any details you can about the vehicle. Look for nearby security cameras and point them out to the responding officers. Check whether you carry uninsured motorist (UM) coverage on your own auto policy, as this is your primary source of compensation when the driver can't be identified.
  </Question>

  <Question q="How long do I have to file a claim after a pedestrian crash in Arizona?">
    The general statute of limitations is two years from the date of the crash. If your claim involves a government entity (like a dangerous road design case), you must file a notice of claim within 180 days. Key evidence like surveillance footage can disappear within 48 to 72 hours, so starting early matters.
  </Question>

  <Question q="Does my car insurance cover me if I'm hit while walking?">
    In many cases, yes. If you have uninsured/underinsured motorist (UM/UIM) coverage on your auto policy, it can apply when you're struck as a pedestrian. MedPay coverage on your auto policy can also cover your medical bills regardless of fault. Review your policy or ask your attorney what applies.
  </Question>

  <Question q="How much is a pedestrian injury case worth?">
    Every case is different. The value depends on the severity of your injuries, your medical costs, your lost income, the impact on your daily life, and the degree of fault assigned to the driver. Pedestrian crashes often involve severe injuries (TBI, broken bones, internal damage), which typically result in higher compensation than many vehicle-on-vehicle crashes. Your attorney can give you a realistic range after reviewing your medical records and the facts.
