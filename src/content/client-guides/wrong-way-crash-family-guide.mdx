Wrong-way crashes are among the most violent collisions on Arizona roads. They're almost always head-on. The combined speed of two vehicles moving toward each other means the force of impact is devastating. Survival rates are lower. Injuries are catastrophic. And in most cases, the wrong-way driver was impaired.

If you survived a wrong-way crash, or if you lost a family member in one, this guide walks through what comes next. The evidence you need to preserve. The legal claims available to you. The timelines that matter. And the support you'll need along the way.

Our [investigation into wrong-way crashes on Arizona highways](/insights/wrong-way-crashes-arizona-highways/) found that despite $4 million in technology deployments, wrong-way fatalities haven't declined. The pattern is consistent: impaired drivers, late-night hours, freeway on-ramps, and head-on collisions at highway speed. Understanding that pattern helps your case.

## If You Survived a Wrong-Way Crash

You're alive. That's not a given in these collisions. The injuries you're dealing with are likely severe: traumatic brain injury, spinal damage, internal bleeding, broken bones, and crush injuries are common.

**Stay still at the scene if you suspect head, neck, or back injuries.** Wrong-way crashes produce enormous deceleration forces. Moving with an undiagnosed spinal injury can cause permanent damage. Wait for paramedics.

**Call 911 if you're able.** If someone else has already called, stay where you are and wait. The responding officers will handle the scene, test the wrong-way driver for impairment, and generate a crash report.

**Don't talk to the other driver or their passengers.** Emotions run high after a wrong-way crash, especially if impairment is obvious. Don't confront anyone. Let law enforcement handle it. Anything said at the scene can complicate the legal process later.

**If you can use your phone, photograph everything.** The vehicles. The point of impact. The road. The other driver's license plate. Any visible bottles, cans, or containers in or around the wrong-way vehicle. The highway signs and on-ramp if visible. Skid marks or the absence of them (impaired drivers often don't brake).

## If a Family Member Didn't Survive

The shock is immediate and total. Nothing prepares you for this call.

In the first days, focus on yourself and your family. Eat something. Sleep when you can. Accept help from the people around you. The legal process can wait a few days. The evidence can't, but your attorney handles that part.

Here's what needs to happen soon.

**Request an autopsy report.** The medical examiner's office performs autopsies in all fatal crashes. The report documents cause of death, severity of injuries, and may include toxicology results for your family member (to establish they weren't impaired). Request a copy when it's available.

**Obtain certified copies of the death certificate.** You'll need five to ten copies. Your funeral director can help you order them through the Arizona Department of Health Services or the county vital records office.

**Contact a wrongful death attorney.** This is the step that protects everything else. Your attorney preserves evidence, communicates with insurance, and begins investigating all potentially responsible parties. Most wrongful death consultations are free, including ours.

**Don't talk to any insurance company.** The wrong-way driver's insurer will contact you. They may sound compassionate. Their job is to settle cheaply and close the file. Decline to discuss details and tell them your attorney will be in touch.

<Callout type="warning" title="The one-year dram shop deadline">
If the wrong-way driver was drinking at a bar, restaurant, or licensed establishment before the crash, Arizona's dram shop law (ARS 4-312) lets you bring a claim against that business. But the statute of limitations is just one year. Not two. One. If your attorney doesn't investigate the driver's drinking location immediately, this claim can expire before anyone realizes it exists.
</Callout>

## Investigating Dram Shop Liability

Most wrong-way crashes involve alcohol. The question your attorney will ask is: where was the driver drinking?

Arizona's dram shop law holds bars, restaurants, and liquor stores liable when they serve alcohol to someone who is obviously intoxicated and that person causes injury or death. The legal standard is whether the establishment served the driver when the driver was "obviously intoxicated" (ARS 4-311 and 4-312).

Here's what the investigation looks like.

**Toxicology and BAC results.** Law enforcement draws blood from the wrong-way driver at the scene or at the hospital. The blood alcohol content establishes the level of impairment. A BAC of 0.15 or higher (roughly twice the legal limit) suggests the driver consumed a significant amount of alcohol over a period of time. Someone served them.

**Credit card and debit card records.** Your attorney can subpoena the driver's financial records to identify where they purchased drinks. A charge at a bar two hours before the crash is powerful evidence.

**Bar receipts and surveillance footage.** Once the establishment is identified, your attorney sends a preservation letter and eventually subpoenas their records. How many drinks were served? Over what time period? Did the staff continue serving after the driver showed signs of impairment? Security camera footage often answers these questions directly.

**Witness interviews.** Bartenders, servers, other patrons. Did anyone notice the driver was intoxicated before they left? Did the staff attempt to cut them off? Did anyone try to stop them from driving?

**Rideshare and phone records.** Did the driver attempt to call a rideshare and cancel? Did someone offer them a ride? Phone records can establish the driver's state of mind and the establishment's opportunity to intervene.

Dram shop claims matter because bar and restaurant insurance policies often have higher limits than the individual driver's auto policy. In cases with catastrophic injuries or wrongful death, the driver's auto insurance may not come close to covering the damages. The dram shop claim fills that gap.

For a deeper look at Arizona's dram shop and social host liability rules, read our [legal guide on dram shop liability](/legal-guides/arizona-dram-shop-liability/).

## Insurance Claims When the Wrong-Way Driver Was Impaired

Insurance in wrong-way crash cases can be complicated. Here's what usually happens.

**The wrong-way driver's auto liability insurance.** This is the primary source of compensation. Arizona's minimum liability coverage is $25,000 per person. Many impaired drivers carry only the minimum or, worse, no insurance at all. In a wrong-way crash with severe injuries, $25,000 doesn't come close.

**Your own UM/UIM coverage.** If the wrong-way driver was uninsured or underinsured, your own uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage fills the gap. This is coverage you paid for on your own policy. It applies when the at-fault driver can't cover your damages.

**The dram shop establishment's insurance.** If dram shop liability is established, the bar or restaurant's commercial liability policy becomes an additional source of compensation. These policies are typically much larger than personal auto policies.

**Punitive damages.** Arizona allows punitive damages in cases involving willful misconduct or gross negligence. A driver who chose to drink excessively and then drove the wrong way on a highway may meet that standard. Punitive damages aren't covered by insurance. They come from the defendant personally. In practice, they're most relevant in dram shop cases or cases involving repeat DUI offenders with assets.

**MedPay on your own policy.** If you carry Medical Payments coverage, it pays your medical bills quickly regardless of fault. MedPay is typically $1,000 to $10,000 and doesn't require proving who was at fault.

## Preserving Evidence

Wrong-way crash evidence is some of the most time-sensitive in personal injury law. Here's what your attorney should preserve in the first 24 to 48 hours.

**BAC and toxicology results.** Law enforcement handles the blood draw, but your attorney ensures the results are preserved and disclosed. Defense attorneys sometimes challenge blood draw procedures. Having the full chain of custody documented from the start prevents surprises.

**Bar and restaurant records.** Receipts, credit card transactions, surveillance footage, server notes. Your attorney sends preservation letters to any establishment the driver may have visited.

**Dashcam footage.** Both from the wrong-way driver's vehicle (if equipped) and from any vehicles that witnessed the crash. Some commercial vehicles on the highway may have captured the wrong-way driver traveling against traffic.

**Highway camera footage.** ADOT operates cameras and wrong-way detection systems on certain highway segments. Your attorney can request footage and detection system logs showing when the wrong-way driver entered the highway and how far they traveled before the collision.

**The driver's phone.** Phone records show whether the driver was using the phone before the crash. They also show call history, text messages, and rideshare app activity that might be relevant to the dram shop investigation.

**The wrong-way driver's criminal record.** Prior DUI convictions matter. A repeat offender faces enhanced criminal penalties and may support a punitive damages claim in the civil case.

<Callout type="important" title="Your attorney should send preservation letters within 24 hours">
Bars overwrite surveillance footage on short loops. Credit card records can be purged. Even law enforcement evidence can become harder to access if not formally preserved early. The preservation letter puts every potentially responsible party on legal notice that they must save all relevant records. Destroying evidence after receiving a preservation letter exposes them to court sanctions.
</Callout>

## The Criminal Case and Your Civil Claim

The criminal case and your civil case are separate. They run on parallel tracks and address different things.

**The criminal case** is between the state of Arizona and the wrong-way driver. The county attorney files charges. These may include DUI, aggravated DUI, endangerment, manslaughter, or negligent homicide depending on the facts. The criminal case can result in prison time, fines, and license revocation. It doesn't result in compensation for you or your family.

**Your civil claim** is between you (or your family) and the at-fault parties. This is where you recover compensation for medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, and other damages. You don't have to wait for the criminal case to resolve before pursuing your civil claim. Most attorneys pursue both tracks simultaneously.

As a victim, you have rights in the criminal case under Arizona's Victim's Bill of Rights (Arizona Constitution, Article 2, Section 2.1 and ARS 13-4401). You have the right to be notified of hearings, to be present at trial and sentencing, and to be heard at sentencing. Your attorney can help you exercise these rights.

## Getting Support

Wrong-way crashes, especially fatal ones, leave families in a state of shock that can last months. In our experience, the families who do best are the ones who accept help early.

**Grief counseling.** Losing a family member in a violent crash creates trauma that goes beyond normal grief. A therapist who specializes in traumatic loss can help you and your family process what happened. If children are involved, a child psychologist experienced in sudden loss is worth finding. The cost of counseling is a recoverable damage in your claim.

**Victim advocacy.** The Maricopa County Attorney's Office has a victim services division that can connect you with resources, keep you informed about the criminal case, and help you navigate the system.

**Financial guidance.** If the person who died was the primary earner, the financial impact is immediate. Don't make major financial decisions in the first 30 days. Your attorney can help you understand the timeline for compensation and connect you with resources to bridge the gap.

## Key Deadlines

**Two years for personal injury.** ARS 12-542 gives you two years from the crash date to file suit.

**Two years for wrongful death.** ARS 12-611 gives the family two years from the date of death. If the crash victim survived for a period before passing, the clock starts on the date of death, not the crash date.

**One year for dram shop claims.** ARS 4-312 gives you one year to bring a claim against the bar or restaurant that served the impaired driver. This is the shortest and most commonly missed deadline in wrong-way crash cases.

**180 days for government entity claims.** If the highway design, signage, or wrong-way detection system was defective, a notice of claim against the responsible government entity must be filed within 180 days (ARS 12-821.01).
