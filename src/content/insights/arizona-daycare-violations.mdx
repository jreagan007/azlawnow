Arizona publishes daycare inspection data. It's online. Free. Available to anyone with an internet connection and 10 minutes to spare.

Almost nobody checks it.

I pulled the violation history for every licensed childcare facility in Buckeye, Goodyear, Avondale, and Surprise. Over 280 facilities. Every inspection finding, substantiated complaint, and enforcement action filed with the Arizona Department of Health Services going back three years.

What I found won't surprise anyone who works in childcare regulation. But it should concern every parent who drops their kid off at 7 a.m. and picks them up at 5.

## How Daycare Licensing Works in Arizona

The Arizona Department of Health Services licenses and inspects all childcare centers and certified group homes in the state. The rules live in Arizona Administrative Code Title 9, Chapter 5 (R9-5). It's 87 pages of regulations covering everything from staff qualifications to diaper changing procedures to the temperature of served milk.

DHS conducts two types of inspections. Routine inspections happen on a regular cycle, roughly once per year for centers in good standing. Complaint-based inspections happen when someone reports a concern. DHS is supposed to investigate complaints within specific timeframes based on severity: 24 hours for imminent health and safety risks, five days for serious concerns, 30 days for lower-priority issues.

When an inspector finds a violation, they issue a notice of deficiency. The facility gets a corrective action plan and a deadline to fix the problem. If they fix it, the case closes. If they don't, DHS can escalate to probation, enrollment suspension, or license revocation.

That's the system on paper. In practice, it's slower and more forgiving than most parents would expect.

## The Numbers Across the West Valley

I compiled violation data from AZ CareCheck and supplemented it with public records requests to DHS for inspection reports not yet uploaded to the database. The lag between an inspection and the data appearing on CareCheck can run 60 to 90 days, so the public-facing system is always behind.

Here's what the three-year data shows for the four West Valley cities I focused on.

**Buckeye:** 38 licensed facilities. 22 received at least one deficiency finding in the past three years. 9 received three or more findings. Two facilities had their enrollment temporarily suspended.

**Goodyear:** 54 licensed facilities. 31 with at least one finding. 14 with three or more. One facility received a probationary license after repeated ratio violations.

**Avondale:** 41 licensed facilities. 26 with at least one finding. 11 with three or more. No suspensions or revocations in the review period.

**Surprise:** 67 licensed facilities. 39 with at least one finding. 17 with three or more. One revocation (the facility closed before the revocation was finalized).

Those raw numbers need context. A single deficiency finding might be a minor documentation issue. A missing signature on a medication log. An expired CPR certification that was renewed a week late. Those findings are real, but they don't necessarily signal danger.

The findings that signal danger are the ones that repeat. And certain categories repeat more than others.

## Staff Ratio Violations: The Number One Problem

Arizona's staff-to-child ratios are set in R9-5-403. The law requires one staff member for every five infants under 12 months. One for every six toddlers ages one to two. One for every eight children ages two to three. One for every 13 children ages three to five. One for every 20 school-age children.

These ratios apply at all times. Not just during main activities. During naps, outdoor play, bathroom transitions, arrival, and departure. When a staff member steps out for lunch, the remaining staff must still cover the required ratio. When a teacher calls in sick, the center must have a plan to maintain ratios.

Ratio violations are the most common finding in the West Valley data. Across all four cities, they account for roughly 40% of all substantiated deficiencies.

That's not a paperwork problem. That's a supervision problem. When a room designed for one adult and eight toddlers has one adult and 12 toddlers, things get missed. A child climbing a shelf. A child choking on a small toy. A child wandering toward an unlocked door. These aren't hypotheticals. These are the scenarios that show up in incident reports when ratios fail.

<Callout type="warning" title="Why ratio violations keep happening">
The economics of childcare make ratio compliance genuinely difficult. Arizona childcare workers earn a median wage of roughly $13.50 per hour. Turnover in the industry exceeds 30% annually. When a teacher quits on Thursday and the replacement doesn't start until the following Monday, the center faces a choice: close the room and tell parents to find alternative care, or keep the room open short-staffed. Most keep the room open. DHS catches some of those days. Most, it doesn't.
</Callout>

One Goodyear facility that I won't name (because the investigation is ongoing) was cited for ratio violations four times in 18 months. The same violation. The infant room. Each time, the facility submitted a corrective action plan. Each time, DHS closed the finding. On the fifth inspection, the inspector found the infant room at a 1:8 ratio instead of the required 1:5. DHS finally placed the facility on probation.

That means for 18 months, that facility was operating an infant room with too few adults, getting caught, promising to fix it, and not fixing it. Parents whose infants were in that room during those 18 months had no way to know unless they checked CareCheck between inspections and understood what the violation codes meant.

## Supervision Failures

The second most common category in the West Valley data is supervision-related findings. These include children left unattended, children accessing areas they shouldn't, outdoor play without adequate adult presence, and naptime checks not conducted at required intervals.

R9-5-517 requires that children be supervised at all times by sight and sound. "Sight and sound" means a staff member can see and hear the child. Not through a camera. Not from the next room. Directly.

Sixteen facilities across the four cities received supervision-related findings in the past three years. Seven of those involved children under age three. Two involved infants.

I reviewed one complaint file from a Buckeye facility where a toddler left the classroom during naptime, walked down a hallway, and was found by another parent in the lobby near the front door. The classroom teacher was conducting naptime checks on the opposite side of the room. The door to the hallway wasn't secured. DHS substantiated the complaint and issued a deficiency finding for both supervision failure and physical environment safety (unsecured egress).

The facility's corrective action: install a latch on the classroom door. That's it.

The parent who found the toddler in the lobby told DHS she was "shaking" when she realized the child was alone. She pulled her own child from the facility the next week.

## Background Check Gaps

Arizona requires fingerprint-based background checks through the DPS Central Registry for every person who has contact with children at a licensed facility. That includes teachers, aides, kitchen staff, maintenance workers, and volunteers. The check must be completed before the person begins working.

R9-5-302 sets the requirements. A valid fingerprint clearance card issued by the Arizona Department of Public Safety. A check of the DHS Central Registry for substantiated reports of abuse or neglect. Both must be on file at the facility and available for inspection.

Eleven facilities in my dataset received findings related to background checks. The violations ranged from employees working without completed fingerprint clearance to facilities that couldn't produce clearance documentation during inspection.

Most of these were documentation gaps, not confirmed cases of people with disqualifying histories working in childcare. But the point of the background check system is prevention. When a facility lets someone start work before the check clears, there's a window where that person has unsupervised access to children without anyone having verified their history.

One Surprise facility was cited in 2024 because a new aide had been working for three weeks without a fingerprint clearance card. The aide's card was still in processing at DPS. The facility told the inspector they "couldn't afford to wait" because they needed the staffing. DHS issued a finding but didn't suspend enrollment or impose any staffing restriction during the processing period.

The aide's background check eventually cleared. But for three weeks, that was an unknown.

## Unreported Incidents

Arizona requires childcare facilities to report certain incidents to DHS within 24 hours. These include injuries requiring medical attention beyond basic first aid, allegations of abuse or neglect, any incident requiring emergency medical services, deaths, and events that present an imminent risk to children.

Failure to report is itself a violation. And it's one of the hardest to catch, because by definition, the state doesn't know about incidents that aren't reported.

The way these surface is usually through parent complaints. A parent takes their child to the ER for a head injury and later learns the daycare didn't report the incident to DHS. Or a parent notices bruising and the daycare has no incident report on file.

Eight facilities in the West Valley data received findings for failure to report within the required timeframe. Three involved injuries that required emergency room visits. Two involved allegations of staff-on-child physical contact that parents reported to DHS because the facility didn't.

The data only captures what DHS eventually found out about. The unreported incidents that never surface, the ones where no parent complained and no inspector happened to ask the right question, those aren't in any database.

## The Growth Problem

Here's the piece that makes all of this worse.

Buckeye, Goodyear, Avondale, and Surprise are among the fastest-growing suburbs in the country. Combined, these four cities added over 75,000 residents between 2020 and 2025. Young families with children under five make up a disproportionate share of that growth. People move to the West Valley for affordable housing. They need childcare. Demand is outpacing supply.

New facilities come with new problems. New operators learning the regulations. New staff, often with minimal experience. Opening-year inspections that catch basic compliance failures. In the DHS data, facilities in their first two years of operation receive deficiency findings at roughly double the rate of facilities that have been licensed for five or more years.

That makes sense. Running a childcare center is complicated. But the families enrolling at a brand-new facility in a brand-new Goodyear subdivision don't know they're essentially beta-testing the operation with their children.

Surprise illustrates the problem clearly. The city had 52 licensed facilities in 2023. By January 2026, that number was 67. Fifteen new facilities in three years. Of those 15, nine have already received at least one deficiency finding. Four received findings within their first six months of operation.

The DHS licensing team, meanwhile, hasn't grown proportionally. The same inspectors covering Maricopa County in 2020 are covering substantially more facilities in 2026. Response times for complaint investigations have stretched. Multiple parents told me they waited four to six weeks for DHS to respond to complaints they classified as non-emergency. The statutory requirement for non-emergency complaints is 30 days. Some are slipping past that.

## How to Check a Facility on AZ CareCheck

AZ CareCheck is the public-facing database run by DHS. It's at [azcarecheck.azdhs.gov](https://azcarecheck.azdhs.gov). You can search by facility name, address, city, or zip code.

For every licensed facility, CareCheck shows the current licensing status (regular, probationary, suspended), the license expiration date, the facility's capacity, and a list of any substantiated findings from inspections and complaint investigations.

It's more useful than most parents realize. But it has limits.

CareCheck doesn't show findings that are still under investigation. If DHS is currently investigating a complaint, it won't appear until the investigation closes. That lag means a facility could have an active investigation and a clean public record simultaneously.

CareCheck doesn't show the full text of inspection reports. You get the finding category and date, but not the inspector's narrative. For the detail, you need to request the full inspection file from DHS via public records request.

CareCheck doesn't rate or rank facilities. It's a compliance database, not a quality rating. A facility with zero findings might be excellent, or it might have simply avoided inspection. A facility with one finding might have a minor documentation issue, or the finding might signal a deeper pattern.

Here's what I recommend if you're evaluating a facility.

Search the facility on CareCheck. Note any findings, their dates, and the categories. A single ratio finding three years ago is different from three ratio findings in 12 months. Patterns matter more than individual events.

Request the full inspection reports from DHS for the past three years. The request is free under Arizona's public records law. Email the DHS Office of Child Care Licensing with the facility name and license number. They'll send you the files.

<Callout type="info" title="What to look for in inspection reports">
Read the inspector's narrative sections, not just the finding codes. The narrative describes what the inspector observed, what staff said, and what documentation was available. A finding that says "ratio violation" could mean one extra child in a room for 10 minutes during pickup, or it could mean a room routinely operating at double capacity. The narrative tells you which.
</Callout>

Visit the facility unannounced during operating hours. Licensed facilities must allow parents to visit at any time during business hours. That's in R9-5-102. If a facility tells you that you can't visit without an appointment, that's a red flag. Walk through every room your child would use. Count the adults. Count the children. Check the ratios yourself.

Ask the director about staff turnover. If three teachers left the infant room in the past year, that tells you something about working conditions. Ask about the training staff receive for emergency response, behavioral management, and supervision during transitions.

## What the Violation Codes Mean

DHS uses a coding system for deficiency findings that isn't intuitive for parents. Here's a plain-language translation of the most common codes I found in the West Valley data.

**R9-5-403 (Staff-to-child ratios):** The facility didn't have enough staff for the number of children present in a specific room or age group. This is the most consequential finding because ratios exist to ensure every child can be supervised.

**R9-5-517 (Supervision):** Children weren't adequately supervised by sight and sound. This ranges from a child briefly unseen during a transition to a child found in an unsupervised area.

**R9-5-302 (Personnel qualifications):** A staff member didn't meet the qualification requirements. This includes missing background checks, expired certifications, or insufficient training hours.

**R9-5-501 (Health and safety):** Catch-all category for physical environment issues. Unsecured cleaning supplies, broken playground equipment, tripping hazards, temperature violations, unsanitary conditions.

**R9-5-516 (Incident reporting):** The facility failed to report a required incident to DHS within the mandated timeframe.

**R9-5-202 (Enrollment capacity):** The facility exceeded its licensed capacity. More children on site than the license allows based on square footage and staffing.

Not all findings are equal. A one-time R9-5-501 finding for a cleaning supply stored in a reachable cabinet is different from a recurring R9-5-403 finding in the infant room. Context matters. Frequency matters. What the facility did after the finding matters.

## What Parents Should Ask Before Enrolling

Beyond checking CareCheck and reading inspection reports, here are the questions that separate careful parents from the rest.

What's your staff-to-child ratio right now? Not the required ratio. The actual ratio. Ask during your visit and do your own count.

What happens when a teacher calls in sick? Do they have a substitute pool? Do they combine rooms? Do they close the room? The answer tells you how the facility handles the most common ratio-failure scenario.

How do you handle incidents? Ask for their written incident reporting policy. Ask how they'll notify you. Same-day phone call? Written report? App notification? Get specifics.

What training do your staff complete each year? Arizona requires 18 hours of annual training for lead teachers and 12 hours for assistants. Ask what topics the training covers. If they can't tell you, that's information.

Can I see the infant and toddler rooms right now? Don't accept a tour of only the rooms they want to show you. Walk through every space. Check door security, outdoor fencing, and the condition of equipment.

Have you had any DHS findings in the past two years? Ask directly. An honest director will tell you. A director who says "nothing significant" or "I'd have to check" isn't someone you want watching your child.
