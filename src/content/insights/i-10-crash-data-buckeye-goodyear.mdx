I drive I-10 through Buckeye three or four times a week. Most of the time it's fine. You get used to the speed, the trucks, the way traffic bunches up near the MC 85 interchange. Then one morning you pass a tarp on the shoulder and a fire truck blocking two lanes, and you remember what this road actually is.

It's one of the deadliest corridors in Arizona. The data proves it. And it's getting worse.

ADOT published its 2024 Crash Facts report in early 2025. I went through the numbers, intersection by intersection, for the I-10 corridor between Verrado Way and Estrella Parkway. What I found isn't surprising if you drive this road. But seeing it laid out in a spreadsheet hits different.

## The Highway That Connects Us Also Kills Us

The Papago Freeway segment of I-10 runs roughly 30 miles through the West Valley, from the Loop 303 interchange near Goodyear all the way past Buckeye's city limits toward Tonopah. It carries somewhere between 80,000 and 130,000 vehicles per day depending on the segment. ADOT's traffic count stations confirm those numbers.

That volume alone would make it dangerous. But I-10 through Buckeye and Goodyear has specific problems that other stretches don't.

The speed limit is 65 mph inside Goodyear and jumps to 75 mph west of Estrella Parkway. Trucks run at 65 across both zones. That speed differential creates compression points where passenger vehicles stack up behind semis and then try to pass in tight windows.

There's no median barrier on long stretches west of SR-85. Cross-median crashes are the most deadly crash type on any highway, and ADOT's data shows multiple fatal cross-median events on I-10 between 2020 and 2024.

On January 2, 2024, a wrong-way driver hit a pickup head-on near milepost 112 west of Buckeye. Two people died. On March 15, 2024, a semi rear-ended a stopped vehicle in a construction zone near Sarival Avenue. One fatality. On August 9, 2024, a multi-vehicle pileup near Cotton Lane during a dust storm killed three.

Those aren't outliers. They're the pattern.

## The Numbers ADOT Publishes (And What They Mean)

ADOT's Arizona Crash Information System tracks every reportable crash statewide. A crash is reportable if it involves a fatality, an injury, or property damage above $2,000. Police agencies submit crash reports. ADOT codes them and publishes annual summaries.

Here's what the 2024 data shows for Maricopa County.

**88,094** total reportable crashes. That's 241 per day. One every six minutes.

**560** fatalities. Up from 512 in 2023 and 489 in 2022. Three consecutive years of increases.

**34,297** injury crashes. That's crashes where at least one person was transported or treated at the scene.

**1,247** crashes involving a commercial vehicle (semis, tankers, flatbeds). Maricopa County accounts for nearly 40% of all commercial vehicle crashes statewide.

For I-10 specifically, the corridor between Loop 303 and the Hassayampa River bridge logged 847 reportable crashes in 2024. That's roughly 2.3 per day across a 30-mile stretch.

Of those 847 crashes, 37 were fatal. That's a fatality rate of 4.4%, nearly double the countywide average of 0.6%.

<Callout type="warning" title="Why the fatality rate matters">
A 4.4% fatality rate means that roughly 1 in 23 crashes on this stretch of I-10 kills someone. Countywide, that number is 1 in 157. The difference comes down to speed. At 75 mph, the energy in a collision is more than double what it is at 55 mph. Physics doesn't negotiate.
</Callout>

## Hazen Road and SR-85: The Intersection That Should Have Been Fixed

If you drive west on I-10 past Goodyear, SR-85 is the major interchange. It connects I-10 to I-8, runs south through Gila Bend, and handles a significant share of truck traffic moving between Phoenix and Yuma.

The interchange itself isn't the worst problem. It's the surface streets feeding into it.

Hazen Road crosses SR-85 about a quarter mile south of the I-10 ramps. It's a two-lane road that serves residential neighborhoods on the east side and agricultural land on the west. The intersection has a traffic signal, but the geometry is wrong. Drivers coming off SR-85 at highway speed hit a light they don't expect. Drivers on Hazen Road entering the intersection face traffic coming from three directions at three different speeds.

Between 2020 and 2024, ADOT's crash database shows five fatal collisions at or within 500 feet of the Hazen Road and SR-85 intersection. Seven people died.

Two of those crashes involved left-turning vehicles struck by through traffic on SR-85. One involved a pedestrian crossing Hazen Road at night with no crosswalk and no street lighting. One was a T-bone collision where a driver ran the red signal. One was a semi that failed to stop and pushed a sedan into the intersection.

I pulled the Maricopa Association of Governments' Top 100 Intersections report for 2023. Hazen and SR-85 ranked 34th for total crash frequency countywide. That puts a rural-suburban intersection with maybe 15,000 daily vehicles in the same category as major Phoenix intersections carrying five times the traffic.

The crash rate per million entering vehicles at Hazen and SR-85 is over three times the state average for signalized intersections. That's the number that matters. Raw crash counts favor high-volume intersections. But when you normalize for traffic volume, Hazen and SR-85 stands out as genuinely dangerous.

ADOT approved a safety study for the SR-85 corridor in 2023. The study recommended "geometric improvements" at Hazen Road. As of April 2026, construction hasn't started.

## Broadway Road and SR-85

Three miles south of Hazen Road, Broadway Road crosses SR-85 with a similar setup. Traffic signal. Mixed-speed traffic. Agricultural trucks turning onto a state highway where passenger vehicles are doing 55 to 60 mph.

The crash numbers here aren't as extreme as Hazen Road, but the pattern is identical. Between 2020 and 2024, ADOT data shows 23 reportable crashes at Broadway and SR-85. Three involved serious injuries. One was fatal.

The fatal crash happened in November 2023. A driver heading east on Broadway Road pulled into the SR-85 intersection on a green arrow. A northbound truck on SR-85 ran its red light at approximately 55 mph and struck the driver's door. The driver was pronounced dead at the scene.

What makes Broadway and SR-85 worth flagging isn't just the crash data. It's the growth pattern. Buckeye's southern expansion is pushing residential development closer to these intersections every year. Five years ago, Broadway Road west of SR-85 was mostly farmland. Now there are housing subdivisions within a quarter mile.

More homes mean more daily trips through an intersection that was designed for agricultural traffic volumes. The signal timing, lane configuration, and sight lines haven't changed. The traffic has.

## Why Buckeye's Growth Makes This Worse

Buckeye was the fastest-growing city in the United States by percentage from 2020 to 2024, according to Census Bureau estimates. The city added roughly 30,000 residents in that span, pushing its population past 110,000.

The Teravalis master-planned community alone is approved for 100,000 residents at full buildout. That's not speculation. Douglas Ranch (now Teravalis) broke ground on its first phase. Howard Hughes Holdings has infrastructure permits. The water agreements are signed.

When Teravalis reaches even 25% of planned capacity, that's 25,000 new residents generating an estimated 75,000 additional daily vehicle trips. Most of those trips will touch I-10 at some point.

ADOT's I-10 widening project between Loop 303 and Verrado Way added lanes in 2022 and 2023. That helped. But the widening stops at Verrado Way. West of that point, I-10 narrows back to four lanes (two each direction) through some of the fastest-growing residential areas in the state.

<Callout type="important" title="The construction zone factor">
ADOT has active or planned construction projects on I-10 between Sarival Avenue and the SR-85 interchange through 2028. Construction zones on high-speed highways are among the most dangerous driving environments. ADOT's own data shows that rear-end crashes in construction zones on I-10 increased 34% between 2022 and 2024.
</Callout>

The math isn't complicated. More residents, more vehicles, same road capacity, active construction, and speed limits that top out at 75 mph. The crash numbers will keep climbing unless infrastructure catches up.

Goodyear faces a similar but less acute version of the same problem. The city's population grew from roughly 95,000 in 2020 to over 115,000 in 2024. The I-10 and Bullard Avenue interchange, the I-10 and Litchfield Road interchange, and the I-10 and Dysart Road interchange all show increasing crash frequency year over year in ADOT's data.

## What the Data Doesn't Tell You

ADOT's Crash Facts report is the best public dataset we have. It's also incomplete in ways that matter.

First, it only counts reportable crashes. Fender benders under $2,000 in damage with no injuries don't make the dataset. Industry estimates suggest the actual number of collisions is two to three times higher than the reportable count. That means I-10 through the West Valley probably sees six or seven incidents per day, not 2.3.

Second, the data lags. The 2024 report published in early 2025 uses data submitted by police agencies throughout 2024. Some agencies take weeks or months to submit reports. The Buckeye Police Department and Maricopa County Sheriff's Office both feed into the system, but on different timelines. ADOT acknowledges that the dataset is a snapshot, not a complete record.

Third, severity coding is inconsistent. A crash coded as "possible injury" might involve someone who went to the ER the next day with neck pain that turned into months of treatment. The initial severity code never gets updated. So the data systematically undercounts serious injuries. I've seen crashes coded as "minor injury" where the person ended up in surgery two weeks later.

Fourth, the data doesn't capture long-term outcomes. A "non-fatal injury" crash might involve a traumatic brain injury that changes someone's life permanently. A spinal cord injury. A shattered pelvis that requires two surgeries and a year of rehab. The crash report records what happened at the scene. It doesn't record what happened in the months and years after.

I've talked to people who were in crashes on I-10 that show up in ADOT's data as "possible injury." Some of them are still in physical therapy two years later. Some can't work. Some lost their cars, fell behind on rent, and are still digging out financially. The data point says one thing. Their life says something very different.

Fifth, ADOT's data doesn't break down fault in a way that's publicly accessible. The crash reports themselves contain fault determinations, but the published summaries aggregate everything. You can't easily see, from the public data, how many I-10 crashes were caused by speeding versus distracted driving versus impairment versus mechanical failure.

The Arizona Crash Information System does allow more granular queries, but access requires a registered account and the interface isn't designed for public use. I've used it. It's clunky, but it's the only way to pull intersection-level data without filing public records requests.

Sixth, commercial vehicle data is underreported. The Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration tracks trucking crashes separately, and those numbers don't always align with ADOT's state data. A semi that crosses the median on I-10 and kills two people shows up in both systems, but the contributing factors, the carrier's safety record, the hours-of-service violations that might have caused the crash, none of that shows up in the state database. You have to pull FMCSA records separately.

## What You Can Do If You Crash on I-10

If you're in a crash on I-10 in Buckeye or Goodyear, the first 48 hours matter more than most people realize. What you do right now shapes everything that comes after.

Get medical attention, even if you feel fine. Adrenaline masks pain. Soft tissue injuries, herniated discs, and concussions don't always show symptoms at the scene. An ER visit or urgent care visit within 24 hours creates a medical record that connects your injuries to the crash. Without that record, insurance companies will argue the injuries happened somewhere else. I've seen adjusters deny claims specifically because the person waited three days to see a doctor.

Don't give a recorded statement to the other driver's insurance company. They'll call fast, sometimes within hours. They're not calling to help you. They're calling to get you on record saying something they can use to reduce your claim. "I feel okay" on a recorded line becomes exhibit A when they deny your treatment six weeks later. "I think I might have been going a little fast" becomes their fault argument, even if the other driver ran a red light.

Document everything. Photos of the vehicles from every angle. The road surface. Traffic signals. Weather conditions. Skid marks. Debris. Photos of your injuries the day of the crash and every day for a week after. Screenshots of the location on your phone's map. Get the other driver's insurance information, their license plate, and the responding officer's name and badge number.

If the crash happened in a construction zone, document the construction signage, lane markings, barriers, and any workers or equipment visible. Construction zone crashes on I-10 have increased 34% in two years, and the construction company may bear partial liability if signage was inadequate or lane transitions were unsafe.

Call a lawyer before you call your own insurance company. Arizona follows pure comparative negligence under ARS 12-2505. That means your compensation gets reduced by your percentage of fault. Insurance adjusters on both sides are trained to shift fault onto you. The statute of limitations is two years from the date of death, not the date of the crash. But evidence disappears fast. Skid marks fade in weeks. Surveillance footage from nearby businesses gets overwritten on 30-day loops. Witness memories change. The responding officer's notes are detailed at the time and vague six months later.
