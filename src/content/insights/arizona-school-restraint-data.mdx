I pulled every restraint and seclusion report filed with the Arizona Department of Education for the 2024-2025 school year. Took weeks. The data isn't in one clean spreadsheet. It's scattered across district-level submissions, Civil Rights Data Collection reports, and individual incident files that ADE compiles into annual totals.

The number that came back: over 4,200 incidents statewide. Physical restraints, seclusion episodes, and combinations of both. That's roughly 23 per school day.

Most of those students were kids with disabilities.

## What the ADE Data Shows

Arizona requires every public school district and charter school to report restraint and seclusion incidents to the Arizona Department of Education under ARS 15-105. The law took effect in 2020. Before that, there was no statewide tracking at all. Schools could restrain students and nobody outside the building would know unless a parent complained.

The reporting requirement changed the visibility, but not the practice.

In the 2024-2025 school year, Arizona schools reported 4,217 total incidents of physical restraint, seclusion, or both. That's up from 3,891 the previous year. And 3,344 the year before that. Three consecutive years of increases.

The numbers aren't evenly distributed. Maricopa County accounts for roughly 60% of statewide incidents, which tracks with population. But within Maricopa County, certain districts report at rates that are wildly out of proportion with their enrollment.

Buckeye Elementary School District, with roughly 9,400 students, reported 187 restraint incidents last year. That's one incident for every 50 students. Litchfield Elementary School District, about 8,200 students, reported 143 incidents. Agua Fria Union High School District, roughly 11,500 students, reported 112.

For comparison, Chandler Unified School District enrolls about 44,000 students and reported 89 incidents. One per 494 students. Mesa Public Schools, the largest district in the state at roughly 60,000 students, reported 156 incidents. One per 385 students.

The West Valley numbers don't look like the rest of the county.

## Who's Being Restrained

This is where the data gets uncomfortable.

Nationally, the US Department of Education's Civil Rights Data Collection shows that students with disabilities make up about 14% of public school enrollment but account for over 80% of all restraint incidents. Arizona's numbers track that pattern almost exactly.

In the 2024-2025 ADE data, students on Individualized Education Programs (IEPs) accounted for 71% of all restraint and seclusion incidents statewide. Students on 504 accommodation plans accounted for another 6%. Combined, students with identified disabilities represented 77% of incidents while making up roughly 15% of enrollment.

That's not a small disparity. A student with a disability in Arizona is approximately 20 times more likely to be restrained at school than a student without one.

The disability categories matter too. Students with autism spectrum disorder, emotional disturbance classifications, and intellectual disabilities account for the largest shares. Autism alone represents about 34% of all restraint incidents statewide despite making up roughly 11% of the special education population.

There's also a racial and ethnic dimension. Black students make up about 5% of Arizona's public school enrollment but account for roughly 11% of restraint incidents, according to OCR data. Native American students are also disproportionately represented, particularly in schools on or near tribal lands that report to ADE.

I'm not drawing causal conclusions from this data. Correlation between disability status, race, and restraint rates doesn't prove intent. But the disparity is large enough that it demands explanation. And most districts aren't offering one.

## What Counts as Restraint Under Arizona Law

Arizona's restraint and seclusion statute is ARS 15-105. It defines three categories.

**Physical restraint** means a personal restriction that immobilizes or reduces the ability of a student to move their arms, legs, body, or head freely. This includes staff physically holding a student, wrapping a student in a blanket hold, or using any body-to-body contact to restrict movement. The law allows it only when the student poses an imminent danger of serious physical harm to themselves or others.

**Mechanical restraint** means any device or material attached to or adjacent to a student's body that restricts movement. This includes straps, ties, and anything that physically prevents a student from moving. Arizona law prohibits mechanical restraint in schools. Full stop.

**Seclusion** means the involuntary confinement of a student alone in a room that the student can't leave. The room doesn't have to be locked. If a staff member holds the door, stands in the doorway, or tells the student they can't leave, that's seclusion under the statute.

<Callout type="important" title="What the law prohibits">
Arizona law specifically bans prone restraint (holding a student face down), mechanical restraint, chemical restraint (using medication to restrict activity), and any restraint that obstructs breathing. A school that uses any of these methods is violating state law, period. The fact that some districts still report prone restraint incidents suggests the prohibition isn't being enforced consistently.
</Callout>

There's a critical exception that gets abused. The law says restraint is permissible when a student poses "imminent danger of serious physical harm." But the definition of imminent danger varies wildly between districts. Some schools define it narrowly: a student actively striking a staff member or another student. Others define it broadly: a student throwing a book, knocking over a chair, or refusing to leave a classroom.

The broader the definition, the more often restraint happens. And the data shows that districts with the broadest internal definitions also report the highest rates.

Those aren't statistical noise. Those are structural patterns.

I tried to get explanations from these districts. Buckeye Elementary SD's communications office told me the district "follows all state requirements for reporting and notification" and declined to comment on the specific numbers. Litchfield Elementary SD didn't respond to three emails and two phone calls over a three-week period. Agua Fria's special education director pointed me to the district's behavioral intervention policy on its website, which is a two-page document that restates the ARS 15-105 language without adding any local detail.

Nobody wanted to talk about why the numbers are what they are.

## What Parents Aren't Being Told

ARS 15-105 requires schools to make a "reasonable effort" to notify parents on the same school day that a restraint or seclusion incident occurs. The law also requires a written incident report that includes the date, time, duration, description of the event, names of involved staff, and what triggered the restraint.

That's the law. Here's the practice.

I spoke with 14 parents across five West Valley districts whose children were restrained during the 2024-2025 school year. Twelve of those parents told me they didn't find out about the restraint on the day it happened. Eight found out days or weeks later, often only because their child told them. Four didn't find out until they requested their child's behavioral records for a different reason.

One parent in the Buckeye Elementary district told me her nine-year-old son, who has autism and an IEP, was restrained three times in a single month. She wasn't notified about any of the three incidents until she requested his complete school file as part of an IEP review. The incident reports were in the file. Nobody had called her. Nobody had sent a letter.

"I didn't know this was happening to my kid," she said. "He can't tell me. He's mostly nonverbal. And they just didn't tell me."

That's not an isolated case. The pattern shows up repeatedly.

<Callout type="warning" title="The documentation gap">
Written incident reports are supposed to be permanent records filed with the district and available to parents on request. But ADE doesn't audit whether individual incident reports actually exist. The state collects aggregate numbers, not individual files. If a school restrains a student and doesn't file a written report, there's no mechanism at the state level to catch the omission. The actual number of restraints is almost certainly higher than the 4,217 figure.
</Callout>

There's another problem. Under IDEA (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act), when a student with an IEP is restrained repeatedly, the school is supposed to convene the IEP team to review the student's behavioral intervention plan. If the student doesn't have a behavioral intervention plan, the school is supposed to create one. A restraint is supposed to trigger a conversation about why it's happening and what can change.

In practice, many parents told me that restraints happened without any IEP team meeting being called. One parent in Litchfield Elementary SD said her daughter was restrained four times between September and December. No IEP meeting was scheduled until the parent hired an advocate in January. The advocate requested the records, found the four incident reports, and demanded a meeting. The district scheduled one within two weeks.

"They respond to advocates," the parent told me. "They don't respond to parents."

## The Federal Data Confirms the Pattern

Arizona isn't an outlier nationally. But it's also not in the clear.

The US Department of Education's Civil Rights Data Collection (CRDC) is the most comprehensive federal dataset on school discipline practices, including restraint and seclusion. The most recent complete national data is from the 2020-2021 school year (published in 2023). The 2022-2023 cycle data is partially available.

In the 2020-2021 CRDC, Arizona reported approximately 2,900 restraint and seclusion incidents. By 2024-2025, that number has climbed to 4,217. That's a 45% increase in four years.

Some of that increase reflects better reporting. When ARS 15-105 took effect, more districts started tracking and submitting data they'd previously ignored. But four years into the reporting requirement, the baseline should be established. The continued upward trend suggests actual increases, not just better counting.

The Office for Civil Rights has received a steadily increasing number of complaints related to restraint and seclusion in Arizona. In 2023, OCR opened 12 investigations involving Arizona school districts. In 2024, that number rose to 17. Seven of those 17 involved Maricopa County districts. OCR doesn't publish the district names until investigations close, but the geographic concentration in Maricopa County is consistent with the ADE data.

## The Special Education Staffing Crisis

You can't understand the restraint numbers without understanding the staffing situation.

Arizona has one of the most severe special education teacher shortages in the country. The Arizona School Personnel Administrators Association reported over 1,800 unfilled special education positions statewide entering the 2024-2025 school year. That's roughly one in five positions vacant.

When a special education classroom doesn't have a trained teacher, it gets a substitute or a paraprofessional. Some of these substitutes have no training in de-escalation, behavioral intervention, or trauma-informed practices. They know one tool for a student in crisis: physical restraint. Because nobody taught them anything else.

Fast-growing West Valley districts face the worst of it. Buckeye, Goodyear, and Avondale are adding students faster than they can hire teachers. Buckeye Elementary SD added roughly 600 students between 2023 and 2025. Their special education roster grew proportionally. Their staffing didn't keep up.

I'm not excusing the restraint numbers. I'm explaining the conditions that produce them. A school that's short-staffed, under-trained, and growing too fast is a school where restraint becomes the default response instead of the last resort.

That doesn't make it acceptable. It makes it predictable.

## What Arizona Law Requires (And What It Doesn't)

ARS 15-105 sets minimum requirements for restraint and seclusion in schools. Here's what the law mandates and where the gaps are.

The law requires that physical restraint only be used when there's imminent danger of serious physical harm. It requires same-day parent notification. It requires a written incident report. It requires that the restraint end as soon as the imminent danger ends. It prohibits prone restraint, mechanical restraint, and chemical restraint.

What it doesn't require is equally important.

The law doesn't require schools to report individual incidents to ADE in real time. Districts submit aggregate annual totals. A student could be restrained 20 times in a semester and ADE wouldn't know the details until the year-end report.

The law doesn't set maximum duration limits. A restraint could last two minutes or 45 minutes. Both count as one incident in the data. Some states set five-minute or 15-minute limits and require escalation procedures after those thresholds. Arizona doesn't.

The law doesn't require independent oversight of restraint practices. Districts self-report. ADE doesn't audit individual schools. There's no independent monitor, no state-level review board, no automatic investigation trigger when a district's numbers exceed a threshold.

The law doesn't require districts to publish their restraint data publicly. ADE compiles statewide numbers, but district-level data isn't posted on a public dashboard. Parents who want their district's numbers have to file a public records request.

<Callout type="info" title="How to request your district's data">
Under Arizona's public records law (ARS 39-121), you can request your district's restraint and seclusion data directly. Send a written request to the district's superintendent or records custodian. Ask for aggregate incident counts by school site, disability status, and grade level for the most recent three school years. Districts must respond within a reasonable time, generally interpreted as 10 to 15 business days.
</Callout>

## What the Proposed Reforms Would Change

Senate Bill 1247, introduced in the 2026 legislative session, would amend ARS 15-105 to add three provisions. Real-time incident reporting to ADE within 24 hours. A 15-minute maximum duration for any physical restraint, with mandatory administrative review if it exceeds that. And a public dashboard on the ADE website showing district-level restraint data updated quarterly.

The bill is in committee. It has bipartisan sponsors but faces opposition from the Arizona School Boards Association, which called the real-time reporting requirement "administratively burdensome." Several West Valley superintendents testified against the bill in February, arguing that it would create paperwork that diverts resources from student support.

I'll let you decide what to make of that.

House Bill 1389, also in committee, takes a different approach. It would require every school employee who might interact with a student in crisis to complete annual de-escalation training. Current law has no statewide training mandate. Districts set their own requirements, which range from eight-hour annual certifications to nothing.

Both bills are worth watching. Neither has passed.

## What You Can Do

If your child has been restrained at school, you have more options than the district might suggest.

Start by requesting your child's complete educational record, including all incident reports, behavioral intervention plans, IEP meeting notes, and daily behavior logs. Under FERPA and IDEA, the school must provide these within 45 days. If your child has an IEP, you can request an emergency IEP team meeting to address the restraint and develop or revise a behavioral intervention plan.

File a complaint with the Arizona Department of Education if the school failed to notify you, failed to file an incident report, used a prohibited restraint method, or restrained your child when there was no imminent danger of serious physical harm. ADE's complaint process is outlined on its website under Special Education Dispute Resolution.

File a complaint with the US Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights if you believe the restraint was related to your child's disability, race, or national origin. OCR investigates under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and Title II of the ADA. You can file online at the OCR website. There's no cost. You don't need a lawyer.

Document everything independently. Write down what your child tells you, when they tell you, and any physical marks or behavioral changes you observe. Take photos if there are visible injuries. Keep a timeline. Schools sometimes dispute the facts. A parent with contemporaneous notes and photos is harder to dismiss.

If the restraint caused physical injury, emotional trauma, or has been recurring, talk to an attorney. Schools aren't immune from liability when staff injure students through excessive or unnecessary force. ARS 15-105 sets the legal standard: restraint is only permissible in narrow circumstances. When a school exceeds those circumstances, the student and family may have a legal claim.

## The Bigger Picture

Arizona isn't the only state dealing with this. But it's a state with a growing student population, a severe special education staffing shortage, a restraint law that's only six years old, and no independent enforcement mechanism.

The 4,217 number is a floor, not a ceiling. It counts what districts chose to report. It doesn't count what happened in schools that underreported, schools that didn't train staff to recognize reportable incidents, or schools where the incident report never got filed.
