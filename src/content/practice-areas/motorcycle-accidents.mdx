Motorcyclists die at a rate of 31.39 per 100 million vehicle miles traveled. Car occupants die at 1.13. That's a 28-to-1 ratio. NHTSA's data is clear: riding a motorcycle is roughly 28 times more dangerous per mile than driving a car.

Arizona recorded 257 motorcycle fatalities in 2023. Nationally, 6,335 riders died that year, a record high. The number has climbed every year since 2019. And Arizona's year-round riding weather means exposure is constant, not seasonal.

The [motorcycle fatality report](/investigations/arizona-motorcycle-fatality-report/) covers Arizona-specific crash patterns. In Mesa alone, motorcyclist fatalities accounted for nearly half of all traffic deaths in 2024. Sixteen riders killed in one city in one year.

## The Left-Turn Problem

The single most common fatal motorcycle crash pattern is the left-turn collision. A car turns left at an intersection. The motorcycle is going straight or passing through. The car driver says "I didn't see them."

NHTSA data shows 46% of fatal two-vehicle motorcycle crashes involve this exact scenario. It's the number one killer of motorcyclists. Intersection crashes account for 37% of all fatal motorcycle crashes.

The physics don't help. A motorcycle has a narrower profile than a car. It's harder to judge the speed of an approaching motorcycle than an approaching car. And when the collision happens, the rider has no crumple zone, no airbag, no seatbelt. The rider is the crumple zone.

<Callout type="important" title="Single-vehicle crashes">
35% of all motorcycle fatalities are single-vehicle crashes. No other car involved. Road hazards (gravel, potholes, oil, uneven pavement), excessive speed, and loss of control on curves account for most of these. If a road defect caused the crash, the government entity responsible for road maintenance may be liable, but the 180-day notice of claim deadline under ARS 12-821.01 applies.
</Callout>

## Arizona's Helmet and Lane Laws

Arizona has no universal helmet law. Under ARS 28-964, helmets are required only for riders under 18. Eye protection is required for all riders. Nationally, 35% of motorcyclists killed in 2023 were not wearing helmets.

Insurance companies use helmet non-use aggressively in Arizona. They'll argue under comparative negligence (ARS 12-2505) that not wearing a helmet worsened the injuries. This is a severity argument, not a causation argument. Not wearing a helmet doesn't cause the crash. An attorney can force that distinction in front of a jury.

Lane filtering became legal in Arizona under HB 2794, codified as ARS 28-903.01. But the conditions are narrow: traffic must be stopped or slow-moving, the motorcycle must travel at 15 mph or less, and the posted speed limit must be 45 mph or lower. Lane splitting in flowing traffic remains illegal under ARS 28-903.

## Motorcycle Crash Injuries

Motorcycle crashes produce injuries that are fundamentally different from car crashes. There's no vehicle structure absorbing energy. The rider absorbs it.

Common motorcycle crash injuries include traumatic brain injuries (even with helmets), spinal cord damage, road rash requiring skin grafts, compound fractures (especially pelvis, femur, tibia), internal organ damage, and degloving injuries. Treatment timelines are measured in months and years, not weeks.

Medical costs accumulate fast. Emergency surgery, ICU stays, follow-up surgeries, physical therapy, occupational therapy, prosthetics. A spinal cord injury can generate $500,000 to $5 million in lifetime medical costs. Arizona has no cap on damages, which means the recovery should reflect the actual cost.

## What to Do After a Motorcycle Crash

Get medical attention immediately. Even if you can stand up and walk, the adrenaline is masking injuries that will present in hours. Road rash that looks superficial can hide deep tissue damage. Concussions don't always show symptoms at the scene.

If you're able, document the crash scene. Photos of the motorcycle, the other vehicle, the road surface, any debris, traffic signals, and skid marks. Get the other driver's insurance information and the responding officer's name and badge number.

Don't give a recorded statement to anyone's insurance company before talking to an attorney. The other driver's insurer will try to establish that you were speeding, lane splitting, or not wearing proper gear. Your own insurer may try to limit your uninsured/underinsured motorist claim.

## West Valley Crash Patterns

The West Valley carries more motorcycle risk than most riders realize. Buckeye, Goodyear, Avondale, and Surprise have added population faster than they've added safe-road infrastructure. Arterials like Bell Road, Grand Avenue, Northern Parkway, and MC-85 run 45 to 55 mph with wide lanes, long light cycles, and intersections that funnel turning vehicles across motorcycle paths.

The [I-10 crash data for Buckeye and Goodyear](/investigations/i-10-crash-data-buckeye-goodyear/) shows that the corridor produces motorcycle fatalities at rates well above the state average, driven by the same factors: speed, volume, and driver inattention at intersections.

Arizona's year-round riding weather compounds exposure. Unlike snowbelt states where motorcycle risk is seasonal, Arizona riders are exposed to crash risk every month. That's part of why ADOT recorded 257 motorcycle fatalities in 2023 and why Mesa alone lost 16 riders that year.

## Maricopa County Superior Court and Motorcycle Cases

Motorcycle injury cases with damages over $10,000 are filed in Maricopa County Superior Court. Most serious motorcycle cases clear that threshold in the ER. A compound tib-fib fracture, an ICU stay, or a spinal fusion can produce six-figure medical bills before physical therapy starts. The court handles the civil claim against the at-fault driver, the UM/UIM claim against your own insurer if applicable, and any dram shop claim if alcohol was involved in the crash.

Wrongful death claims from motorcycle fatalities also land in Superior Court under ARS 12-611 through 12-613. Arizona's constitutional prohibition on damage caps (Article 2, Section 31) means the jury can award whatever the evidence supports. That matters enormously in motorcycle fatality cases, where lost future earnings for a primary earner in their 30s or 40s can run into the millions.

## Statute of Limitations and Discovery Rule

The Arizona statute of limitations for motorcycle injury claims is two years from the date of the crash under A.R.S. 12-542. For wrongful death, it's two years from the date of death under A.R.S. 12-611. For claims involving a government vehicle or a road design defect maintained by a government entity, the 180-day notice of claim deadline under A.R.S. 12-821.01 runs first and is the deadline most commonly missed.

If the injury wasn't reasonably discoverable right away, for example a traumatic brain injury that only surfaces with cognitive testing weeks after the crash, Arizona's discovery rule can delay when the clock starts. That rule is narrower than many riders expect. Consulting an attorney early preserves the options.

## Typical Settlement Ranges

Motorcycle crash settlement ranges vary by injury severity and insurance coverage. Cases with minor injuries and a clean liability picture often settle in the $50,000 to $150,000 range. Cases with moderate injuries (surgery, extended physical therapy) run $150,000 to $500,000. Cases with catastrophic injuries (traumatic brain injury, paralysis, amputation) or fatalities regularly exceed $1 million, and the ceiling depends on available insurance, not on what the case is actually worth.
