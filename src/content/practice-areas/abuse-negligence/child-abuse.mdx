Arizona takes child abuse seriously. The state's criminal statutes impose severe penalties on abusers, and the civil justice system gives families a separate path to hold responsible parties accountable. When a child is harmed by someone who was supposed to protect them, the legal response needs to match the severity of what happened.

Brandon Millam and our team handle civil child abuse cases throughout Arizona. We pursue compensation from abusers and from the institutions that let the abuse happen.

## Arizona's Child Abuse Statutes

ARS 8-201 defines the categories of child abuse recognized under Arizona law. Physical abuse covers any non-accidental injury inflicted by a parent, guardian, or custodian. Neglect includes failure to provide food, shelter, clothing, supervision, or medical care. Emotional abuse encompasses patterns of behavior that damage a child's psychological development.

ARS 13-3623 establishes the criminal penalties. Child abuse causing physical injury is a Class 4 felony. When the abuse creates a substantial risk of death or causes serious physical injury, it's a Class 2 felony carrying up to 35 years in prison. These criminal provisions run parallel to civil claims. A family can pursue compensation regardless of whether the state files charges.

## Institutional Liability

Many child abuse cases involve institutions that failed in their duty to protect. Schools that didn't perform background checks on employees. Churches that transferred known offenders to new assignments. Sports programs that ignored complaints from parents. Daycares that left children unsupervised with unvetted workers.

Arizona law holds these institutions accountable when their negligence contributed to the abuse. The legal theory is straightforward: organizations that accept responsibility for children must exercise reasonable care in hiring, supervising, and responding to reports of misconduct. When they don't, they share liability for the resulting harm.

We investigate the institution's hiring practices, training protocols, complaint history, and compliance with mandatory reporting requirements. These records often reveal a pattern of institutional failure that goes beyond any single incident.

## Mandatory Reporting in Arizona

Arizona's mandatory reporting law is among the broadest in the country. Under ARS 13-3620, any person who reasonably believes a child has been abused must report it. This isn't limited to teachers, doctors, or social workers. Every adult in Arizona carries this obligation.

Failure to report known or suspected child abuse is a Class 6 felony. Institutions that discourage reporting or fail to train staff on reporting requirements face additional liability in civil cases.

Reports go to the Department of Child Safety at (888) 767-2445 or to local law enforcement. The report triggers an investigation that creates documentation useful in both criminal and civil proceedings.

## The Statute of Limitations

Arizona provides extended deadlines for child abuse claims. For childhood sexual abuse, survivors can file civil claims until they turn 30. This extended window recognizes that many survivors don't process what happened to them until years later.

For physical abuse and neglect, the standard two-year statute of limitations under ARS 12-542 applies, but the clock doesn't start until the child turns 18. That means survivors have until age 20 to file.

If a government entity is involved, the 180-day notice of claim under ARS 12-821.01 still applies. This shorter deadline can catch families off guard. Contact an attorney early to protect all available claims.
