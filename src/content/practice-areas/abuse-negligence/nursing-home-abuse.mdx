## The Data Tells the Story

Every Medicare-certified nursing home in Arizona is inspected regularly by state and federal surveyors. The results are public. Medicare's Care Compare tool publishes health inspection scores, staffing levels, quality measures, and overall star ratings for every facility.

We start every nursing home case by pulling this data. A facility with repeated citations for infection control, fall prevention, or staffing deficiencies has a documented pattern. That pattern is powerful evidence of systemic neglect, not just a one-time mistake.

Arizona's Department of Health Services also conducts its own inspections and publishes results. Between CMS data and state records, we can build a detailed picture of a facility's compliance history before we ever take a deposition.

## Understaffing Is the Root Cause

Most nursing home neglect traces back to one problem: not enough staff. When a facility operates with fewer nurses and aides than it needs, the math is simple. There aren't enough hands to reposition bedridden residents every two hours. Medications get delayed. Call lights stay on for 20, 30, 40 minutes. Meals arrive cold or late. Residents who need help getting to the bathroom soil themselves because no one comes.

CMS publishes staffing data for every facility, including the ratio of nursing hours per resident per day. We compare that data against the recommended minimums and against the facility's own staffing plans. The gap between what they promised and what they delivered tells the story.

## Types of Nursing Home Abuse

**Physical abuse:** hitting, pushing, rough handling, improper use of restraints. Physical abuse can come from staff members, other residents, or even visitors. Facilities have a duty to protect residents from all sources of harm.

**Neglect:** failure to provide food, water, medication, hygiene, medical care, or supervision. Bedsores are the most visible sign of neglect, but malnutrition, dehydration, and untreated infections are equally serious.

**Emotional abuse:** yelling, threats, intimidation, isolation, or humiliation. Emotional abuse is harder to detect but no less damaging. Watch for changes in your family member's behavior, especially fearfulness or reluctance to speak around certain staff.

**Financial exploitation:** theft of personal belongings, unauthorized charges, or manipulation of financial decisions. Facilities handle residents' personal funds, creating opportunities for misuse.

## The Corporate Shell Game

Many nursing homes in Arizona operate under complex corporate structures. The facility might be owned by one LLC, managed by another company, and staffed through a third-party agency. This fragmentation is intentional. It's designed to shield assets and complicate lawsuits.

We investigate the full ownership and management chain. Every entity that profits from the facility's operation shares responsibility for its failures. We identify them all and hold them accountable.
