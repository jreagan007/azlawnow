Elder abuse is one of the most underreported problems in Arizona. Families trust care facilities and caregivers with their most vulnerable members. When that trust is broken through neglect, physical abuse, or financial exploitation, the harm runs deep. The person suffering may not be able to speak up. They may not even understand what's happening to them.

That's where families step in. And that's where we help.

## Arizona's Elder Abuse Laws

Arizona defines elder abuse under ARS 46-451. The statute covers physical abuse, emotional abuse, neglect, sexual abuse, and financial exploitation of vulnerable adults. A vulnerable adult is anyone 18 or older who is unable to protect themselves from abuse or exploitation due to a physical or mental condition.

The definition of neglect is broad. It includes failure to provide adequate food, water, shelter, clothing, hygiene, medical care, or supervision. A nursing home that's chronically understaffed, a caregiver who skips medications, a facility that ignores bedsores until they become infected. All of these constitute neglect under Arizona law.

## Recognizing the Signs

Abuse and neglect aren't always obvious. The signs can be subtle, especially when the person experiencing them has cognitive impairment or communication difficulties. Watch for these warning signs during visits.

**Physical signs:** unexplained bruises, cuts, or fractures. Bedsores that aren't being treated. Sudden weight loss or dehydration. Poor personal hygiene. Soiled clothing or bedding.

**Behavioral signs:** withdrawal, fearfulness, anxiety around certain staff members, reluctance to speak openly, or unexplained agitation.

**Financial signs:** unexplained withdrawals from bank accounts, changes to wills or powers of attorney, missing personal belongings, or new "friends" who have access to finances.

Trust your instincts. If something feels wrong when you visit, it probably is.

## The Reporting Process

Arizona requires certain professionals, including healthcare workers, social workers, and law enforcement, to report suspected elder abuse. But anyone can report. Contact Arizona Adult Protective Services at (877) 767-2385 or local law enforcement.

Filing a report does two things. First, it triggers an investigation that may provide immediate protection for your family member. Second, it creates an official record that strengthens any future civil claim. We always recommend reporting before or alongside filing a lawsuit.

## Using Public Data to Build Your Case

Medicare's Care Compare tool publishes inspection results, health citations, staffing ratios, and quality ratings for every certified nursing home in Arizona. The Arizona Department of Health Services conducts its own inspections and publishes facility-specific data.

These records often reveal patterns. A facility with repeated staffing violations, infection control citations, or resident safety complaints has a documented history that supports a neglect claim. We review this data as part of every elder abuse investigation.

## Financial Exploitation

Financial abuse of elders is epidemic. Caregivers, family members, and strangers target vulnerable adults who may not understand financial transactions or who can be manipulated through undue influence. Arizona law treats financial exploitation of a vulnerable adult as both a civil wrong and a criminal offense.

Cases involve stolen savings, forged documents, manipulated powers of attorney, and deceptive transfers of property. We work with forensic accountants to trace the money and recover what was taken.
