Arizona is a strict liability state for dog bites. That's the most important thing to understand. Under ARS 11-1025, the dog owner is responsible when their dog bites someone in a public place or lawfully on private property. You don't need to prove the dog had a history of aggression. You don't need to show the owner was careless. The bite happened. The owner pays.

## No One-Bite Rule

Some states follow what's called the "one-bite rule," which essentially gives a dog owner a free pass on the first bite. Arizona rejected that approach entirely. The strict liability statute applies regardless of the dog's prior behavior. A dog that's never shown aggression before is treated the same as a dog with a documented bite history.

This matters because the dog owner's insurance company can't argue they had no reason to know the dog was dangerous. The statute doesn't care about what the owner knew. It cares about what the dog did.

## The One-Year Deadline

Arizona's statute of limitations for dog bite claims is one year from the date of the bite. That's shorter than most personal injury claims, which carry a two-year deadline. One year goes fast, especially when you're dealing with medical treatment, possible surgery, and recovery.

Don't wait. Contact an attorney as soon as possible after a bite so we can preserve evidence, identify insurance coverage, and file within the deadline.

## Children Are the Most Common Families

Children account for the majority of serious dog bite injuries. They're smaller and less able to defend themselves. They tend to approach dogs at face level, which is why bites to the face, head, and neck are far more common in children than adults. These injuries can require plastic surgery, leave permanent scars, and cause lasting psychological trauma.

Courts in Arizona recognize that children interact with animals differently than adults. The provocation defense is extremely difficult to prove against a child. A child who was petting a dog, playing near a dog, or simply walking past a dog isn't provoking the animal in a legal sense.

## Where Dog Bites Happen

The strict liability statute covers bites that happen in public places and on private property where the person is lawfully present. That includes sidewalks, parks, trails, pet stores, and other public areas. On private property, it covers invited guests, delivery workers, postal carriers, repair technicians, and anyone with permission to be there.

In the West Valley, off-leash dogs in neighborhoods, parks, and open desert areas cause frequent incidents. Buckeye, Goodyear, and Surprise all have leash laws that require dogs to be on leash in public spaces. A leash law violation strengthens your case because it's additional evidence of the owner's failure to control their animal.

## Insurance Coverage

Most dog bite claims are paid through the owner's homeowner's or renter's insurance. These policies typically include $100,000 to $300,000 in personal liability coverage. Some policies exclude specific breeds or require additional riders for dogs. We identify the applicable policies early in the process to understand the available coverage.

If the owner doesn't have insurance or the policy limits aren't sufficient, we explore other avenues. Landlords who knew about a dangerous dog may share liability. Property owners who allowed dogs on their premises may be responsible. Every case gets a thorough coverage analysis.

## The Injuries Are Real

Dog bites can cause deep puncture wounds, torn tissue, nerve damage, crushed bones, and severe infections. Facial bites often require plastic or reconstructive surgery. The scarring can be permanent. Beyond the physical injuries, many dog bite survivors, especially children, develop lasting fear and anxiety around animals.

We pursue compensation for all of it: emergency care, surgery, rehabilitation, scar revision, psychological treatment, and the pain and emotional impact of living with the consequences.
