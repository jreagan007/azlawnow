Bus crashes affect more people per incident than almost any other type of vehicle collision. A single crash can injure dozens of passengers, many of them children, elderly riders, or daily commuters who depend on public transit. The injuries range from minor bumps to catastrophic trauma, and the legal process for recovering compensation is more complicated than most people realize.

## The 180-Day Deadline Most People Miss

This is the most important thing to know about bus crash claims in Arizona. When a government-operated bus is involved, you don't get the standard two-year statute of limitations. You must file a notice of claim with the responsible government entity within 180 days under ARS 12-821.01.

That applies to school buses operated by public school districts, Valley Metro buses, city-operated transit, and any other bus run by a government agency. Miss the 180-day window and the law bars your claim entirely. No extensions. No exceptions.

Six months sounds like plenty of time, but it isn't. Medical treatment is ongoing. Records take weeks to compile. The government entity needs to be correctly identified and properly served. Families who wait too long to contact an attorney often find out about this deadline after it's too late.

## School Bus Crashes

When a child gets hurt on a school bus, parents face a confusing mix of government immunity protections, insurance claims, and strict notice deadlines. School districts aren't immune from lawsuits in Arizona, but the process for suing them is different from suing a private party.

Most school buses in Arizona still don't have seatbelts. They rely on compartmentalization, a system of high-backed padded seats designed to cushion passengers in a crash. While effective for certain types of collisions, compartmentalization doesn't protect against side-impact crashes, rollovers, or ejections.

## Valley Metro and Public Transit

Valley Metro operates bus routes throughout the Phoenix metro area, including routes that serve Buckeye, Goodyear, Avondale, and other West Valley communities. Passengers injured on Valley Metro buses face the same 180-day notice requirement as school bus claims.

Public transit crashes can happen when buses rear-end other vehicles, when passengers fall due to sudden stops, or when other drivers collide with the bus. In each scenario, the investigation determines whether the bus driver, the transit authority, another driver, or a combination of parties bear responsibility.

## Charter and Tour Bus Crashes

Private charter and tour bus companies operate under a different legal framework. They're commercial carriers regulated by the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration, similar to trucking companies. They must comply with federal safety regulations, maintain proper insurance, and ensure their drivers meet qualification standards.

When a charter bus crashes, the investigation looks at driver fatigue, vehicle maintenance, safety inspection records, and whether the company has a history of FMCSA violations. These cases often involve higher insurance limits and more complex liability questions than government bus claims.
