Arizona consistently ranks among the most dangerous states for pedestrians. The combination of wide, high-speed roads, limited crosswalks in suburban areas, and year-round warm weather means more people walking and more opportunities for deadly crashes. When a 4,000-pound vehicle hits a person on foot, there's no contest. The human body can't absorb that kind of impact.

## Why Arizona Is So Dangerous for Pedestrians

Most of the Phoenix metro area was designed for cars. Wide arterials with four, five, and six lanes. Speed limits of 45 to 55 mph on surface streets. Long distances between marked crosswalks. Limited sidewalk coverage in newer developments. These design choices prioritize vehicle throughput over pedestrian safety.

The West Valley is especially problematic. Rapid growth in Buckeye, Goodyear, Surprise, and Avondale has added thousands of residents to areas where pedestrian infrastructure hasn't caught up. Roads like MC 85, Indian School Road, and Van Buren Street see regular pedestrian crashes. Some intersections have been flagged by transportation safety advocates for years without meaningful improvements.

## Crosswalk Rules and Right of Way

Arizona law gives pedestrians the right of way in marked crosswalks and at intersections, even unmarked ones. Drivers must yield to pedestrians in these areas. When a driver fails to yield and hits a pedestrian, the driver is almost always at fault.

Outside crosswalks, the picture gets more complicated. Pedestrians have a duty to yield to vehicles when crossing outside a marked crosswalk. But that doesn't mean a driver can hit someone just because they're jaywalking. Drivers always have a duty to exercise reasonable care to avoid hitting pedestrians, regardless of where the pedestrian is walking.

Arizona's pure comparative negligence system means fault can be shared. A pedestrian who crosses against a signal might be found 30% at fault, reducing their recovery by that amount. They still collect the other 70%.

## Hit-and-Run Crashes

Arizona has one of the highest hit-and-run rates in the country. For pedestrians, a hit-and-run is particularly devastating because there's often no one to call for help immediately. The driver disappears, and the injured person is left on the roadside.

When the driver can't be identified, your own uninsured motorist coverage may provide compensation. We also pursue every available avenue to identify the driver: surveillance camera footage from nearby businesses, traffic cameras, witness accounts, and forensic analysis of vehicle debris at the scene.

## Dangerous Road Design Claims

Sometimes the road itself is the problem. Missing crosswalks, inadequate lighting, poorly timed signals, absence of pedestrian refuge islands, and faded road markings all contribute to crashes. When a government entity designed, built, or maintained a road that's unreasonably dangerous for pedestrians, that entity may be liable.

These claims require filing a notice within 180 days under ARS 12-821.01. We investigate crash history data, traffic engineering reports, and prior complaints to build the case that the government knew about the danger and failed to fix it.

## The Injuries Are Severe

Pedestrian crash injuries are among the worst we see. Traumatic brain injuries from hitting the ground or the vehicle's hood. Spinal cord damage. Shattered legs and pelvis. Internal organ damage. Road rash that requires skin grafts. Many of these injuries require multiple surgeries and years of rehabilitation.

We work with medical experts to project the full cost of recovery, including future surgeries, physical therapy, assistive devices, and home modifications. These long-term costs are often the largest component of a pedestrian crash claim.
