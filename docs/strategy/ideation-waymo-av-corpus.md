## Bomb stat (confirmed, citable)

**"Waymo vehicles were involved in at least 202 crashes in Arizona between 2021 and 2024, including 31 that resulted in injuries. The company has issued three fleet-wide recalls in two years, the first one triggered by a collision in a Phoenix alley. In 71 police-documented Arizona crashes where fault was assessed, police attributed the collision to Waymo in 13% of cases. Zero citations have ever been issued."**

Source: NHTSA SGO-2021-01 database (July 2021 to November 2025), as analyzed by azfamily.com (May 2025) and independent legal data firms. Supplemented by NHTSA investigation PE24016 (opened May 13, 2024, targeting 8 AZ incidents in Phoenix and Chandler).

## Why this is Tier S and not Tier A

The Tier-S rubric requires: AZ consumer safety, named regulators and entities, structured-table data, audience extends beyond the legal vertical.

This piece clears every gate:

- **Named entities.** Waymo LLC, NHTSA, ADOT, AZ DPS, City of Phoenix, City of Tempe, Magna International (Mesa production hub).
- **Structured table data.** Three recalls with vehicle counts, six named incidents with dates and locations, ARS statutes with cross-references, per-city crash breakdown.
- **Broad audience.** Every Phoenix metro driver, passenger, and pedestrian has a stake in how Waymo's fleet performs during monsoon season. The May 2026 recall lands five weeks before monsoon season opens, making it a consumer-safety story, not a legal-trade story.
- **Regulatory accountability.** Arizona's enforcement gap (Phoenix PD cannot feasibly cite Waymo; zero citations issued across 71 police-documented crashes) is the kind of systemic finding that consumer, civic, and national press picks up.
- **Historical depth.** Tempe is the site of the first AV pedestrian fatality in US history (Elaine Herzberg, March 2018). Seven years later, a second fatal crash involving a Waymo occurred on the same Tempe corridor (September 2025). The corridor angle matches the BNSF corpus framework already deployed for the railroad pieces.

## Confirmed structural findings

1. **Phoenix is Waymo's largest deployment market and its #2 crash market.** 197 NHTSA-reported incidents in Phoenix alone through November 2025. Tempe adds 93. Scottsdale adds 48. Chandler adds 21. Four AZ cities account for roughly 25% of all Waymo SGO reports nationally.

2. **Three recalls in two years, each triggered by an actual collision.** Recall 1 (672 vehicles, May 2024): Phoenix alley utility pole. Recall 2 (1,212 vehicles, May 2025): stationary barriers. Recall 3 (3,791 vehicles, May 2026): flooded high-speed roadway. NHTSA described the Recall 3 defect rate as 100%.

3. **NHTSA investigation PE24016 singled out 8 AZ incidents.** The investigation opened May 13, 2024, targeting unexpected ADS behaviors including collisions with clearly visible objects and apparent disregard of traffic control devices. Eight of the 22 incidents under federal review were in Phoenix and Chandler.

4. **The flood-recall defect maps directly onto Phoenix monsoon risk.** Phoenix Waymo fleet suspended during September 2025 monsoon. Multiple vehicles got stuck in floodwaters. The May 2026 national recall's permanent fix was still in development as of NHTSA's May 11, 2026 acknowledgment. Monsoon season opens June 15.

5. **Tempe is the historic AV fatality corridor.** Elaine Herzberg died there in 2018. Joseph Gonzalez (18, motorcyclist) died in a Waymo-involved crash near ASU in September 2025. The corridor holds the bookends of the US AV safety story.

6. **Arizona's enforcement framework has a documented gap.** ARS 28-9702 makes the ADS the legal driver. ARS 28-9706 gives ADOT and AZ DPS sole enforcement authority. Phoenix PD has said in writing that citing Waymo is "not feasible." Zero citations issued. The negligence-per-se pathway that normally attaches to documented moving violations doesn't build in the AZ regulatory record.

7. **Mesa is now the production hub.** Waymo is retrofitting 2,000+ Jaguar I-PACE vehicles at the Magna facility in Mesa through 2026, scaling the fleet from ~1,500 to 3,500+. Arizona is simultaneously the largest test market, the second-highest crash market, and the manufacturing base.

**Recommendation: Pillar plus three spokes, sequenced over 60 days.**

**Pillar piece (T=0): The AZ Waymo corpus.** The structural investigation: 202 crashes, 3 recalls, the enforcement gap, the monsoon risk, the Tempe corridor. Byline: Brendan Franks. This is the anchor that earns the Tier-1 press placement and unlocks paid amplification.

**Spoke 1 (T+14): "If a Waymo hits you in Arizona."** The consumer-facing legal guide: how the ARS framework works, how to file a claim, what Waymo's insurance covers, what the comparative fault math looks like. Byline: Brandon Millam J.D. This is the Tier-B SEO piece that captures "Waymo hit my car Arizona" and similar searches. It cross-links to the pillar.

**Spoke 2 (T+30): "Arizona's monsoon season and the AV recall that's still not fixed."** Seasonal news peg piece. Monsoon season opens June 15. The May 2026 flood recall's permanent software fix is still in development. This spoke runs as a Tier-C news update if the fix is deployed, or as a Tier-A investigation if the fix is delayed and incidents recur. Byline: Brendan Franks. Peg: first major monsoon event after June 15.

**Spoke 3 (T+60): "The Tempe corridor: from Elaine Herzberg to Joseph Gonzalez."** The narrative companion. This is the human-centered piece that traces the Tempe AV corridor from 2018 to 2025, using both fatalities as structural bookends and asking what changed in seven years. Person-first throughout. No grievance framing. Structural question: what did Tempe, the state, and the industry do differently between 2018 and 2025, and what didn't change? Byline: Brendan Franks. This is the piece that earns long-read placement at national outlets.

**Rationale for spokes:** The pillar is regulatory. The spoke 1 is the consumer-conversion funnel. The spoke 2 is the seasonal-news capture. The spoke 3 is the national narrative pitch. Together they give Brendan four editorial properties on the same theme with no cannibalization.

## What this piece is NOT

- Not a "Waymo is dangerous" advocacy piece. The data shows Waymo is involved in crashes. What the data also shows is that human drivers are blamed in 87% of police-documented crashes. The structural finding is about the regulatory framework and the enforcement gap, not about demonizing AV technology.
- Not a case solicitation. No named individual client. No "call us if a Waymo hit you." The piece earns referral trust through editorial credibility, not through a body CTA.
- Not a pedestrian-death piece. The pedestrian-death angle belongs to `arizona-pedestrian-deaths-road-design.mdx`. This piece owns the AV-specific regulatory lane.
