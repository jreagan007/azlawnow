## 2. The structural finding

For roughly five decades, the BNSF crossings at 27th Avenue (DOT 025430G) and Thomas Road (DOT 025617C) in west Phoenix carried passive crossbucks. In June 2024, BNSF and the City of Phoenix completed a $\[redacted, see records request\] project that installed two-quadrant gates, new cantilever flashing lights, and traffic signal upgrades at both crossings.

Between the gate retrofit completion and April 25, 2026 (the day Sonja Celius was struck as a pedestrian at 27th Avenue), BNSF filed three Form 6180.57 accident reports at Thomas Road that document two recurring structural defects in the gate configuration:

1. **"AUTO PRECEDED GATES"** (06/15/2025 and 08/07/2025) — vehicles entering the crossing before the gate fully descended. BNSF identified the gate-timing race in writing twice in approximately two months at the same crossing.
2. **"THE AUTO CAME UP AGAINST THE DIRECTION OF TRAFFIC FLOW NOT HAVING GATES"** (12/15/2025) — vehicle entering from a direction with no gate coverage. One passenger injured. BNSF identified the directional-gap defect of the two-quadrant gate configuration in writing 131 days before Sonja's strike.

The mitigations for both failure modes are documented in 49 CFR 222 (Quiet Zone supplementary safety measures) and the Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices Part 8: channelizing medians, four-quadrant gates, pedestrian-specific gates, or wayside horns. None of these were included in the June 2024 retrofit scope per the City of Phoenix project documentation. The current FRA inventory snapshot (Form 6180.71, last updated 7/18/2025) confirms zero channelization devices and zero four-quadrant gates at either crossing.

Twenty-seventh Avenue itself, where Sonja was struck on April 25, 2026, carries the same two-quadrant gate configuration as Thomas Road. The twin crossings sit roughly 50 feet apart on a single BNSF Phoenix Subdivision main, mileposts 189.580 to 189.615.

## 7. The legal-notice framework (for the published piece)

The piece does NOT make a negligence argument. It surfaces the documentary record. Brandon Millam J.D. reviews as legal editor for accuracy on the regulatory framework (49 CFR 222, MUTCD Part 8, FRA Form 6180.57 reporting requirements, 49 USC 20903 evidentiary limitation). The reader can draw their own conclusion. That's editorial discipline.

49 USC 20903 limits the admissibility of Form 6180.57 reports "in any suit or action for damages growing out of any matter mentioned in said report." But the underlying facts (the date, the location, the equipment status, the warning-device codes, the narrative) are independently provable through other means: BNSF dispatcher logs, BNSF engineer event recordings, Phoenix PD CFS, locomotive cab video, witness statements. The Form 6180.57 is journalism's record. The legal admissibility is a separate question for any future litigation.

For Brandon's voice-tone audit: the piece must not say "BNSF was on notice" (legal conclusion language). It can say "BNSF documented the failure mode in writing" (factual). It can say "Four months later, a pedestrian was struck" (factual). It cannot say "BNSF knew" as a conclusion of mind-state. It can say "BNSF documented" or "BNSF wrote" or "the filing identifies." This is the careful editorial discipline that lets the piece publish without crossing into the firm's-own-litigation territory.
