## The Tempe corridor: eight years of AV history

[Writer: keep this section factual and structural. Do not editorialize about either Herzberg or Gonzalez. Person-first throughout. The structural point is that Tempe is the bookend city for AV history in the US and that the corridor has had two separate AV-linked fatalities, seven years apart, with different operators and different circumstances.]

Tempe has served as an AV test corridor since Waymo began driving there in 2017 without a safety driver, one of the first deployments of its kind in the country.

On March 18, 2018, Elaine Herzberg became the first person killed by an AV in the United States, struck by an Uber test vehicle in Tempe. The National Transportation Safety Board investigation found the ADS had detected her six seconds before impact, classified her as an unknown object, then a vehicle, then a bicycle, before determining braking was needed 1.3 seconds before impact. The emergency braking system was disabled during computer-controlled operation.

Arizona revoked Uber's AV permit temporarily. Uber settled with the Herzberg family and eventually exited the AV business.

On September 14, 2025, around 1:20 a.m., a motorcyclist died in a crash near Rural Road and Apache Boulevard in Tempe, close to the ASU campus. Tempe police said the motorcyclist rear-ended a Waymo that was making a right turn and yielding to a pedestrian. Less than a second later, a second vehicle struck the motorcyclist and fled. Tempe police identified the driver of the second vehicle as an ASU student.

Waymo said its vehicle had no passengers and that the company was cooperating fully with law enforcement and regulators.

The decedent's identity has not surfaced in any public source reviewed for this report. NHTSA confirmed in May 2026 that no formal Office of Defects Investigation case has been opened on the September 2025 Tempe fatality. The two open Waymo ODI investigations as of mid-May 2026 are PE25013 (school bus behavior, no injuries) and PE26001 (January 2026 child-injury near elementary school). Neither matches the Tempe crash.

## Arizona's regulatory framework and the enforcement gap

Arizona's AV legal framework is the most permissive in the country by design. Governor Ducey's Executive Order 2018-04 allowed fully autonomous vehicles on public roads with minimal preconditions. The Legislature codified the framework in HB 2813 (2021), now at ARS Title 28 Chapter 32.

Under Arizona law, when a vehicle's ADS is engaged, the system is considered the legal driver (ARS 28-9702). ADOT and AZ DPS are the only state agencies authorized to implement or enforce the AV chapter (ARS 28-9706).

That structure creates a documented gap at the municipal level.

Phoenix Police told AZ Family in May 2025 that citing Waymo for moving violations is "not feasible." Across 71 police-documented Waymo crashes in Arizona where officers assessed fault, police attributed the collision to Waymo in 13% of cases. None resulted in a citation.

## What the law says if a Waymo hits you in Arizona

[Writer: three to four paragraphs max. Cross-link to the companion spoke piece (Spoke 1: if a Waymo hits you in Arizona, Brandon Millam byline) when that piece publishes.]

Arizona's pure comparative fault rules (ARS 12-2505) apply to AV crashes the same as they apply to crashes involving human drivers. Your recovery can be reduced by your percentage of fault, but it's not eliminated.

Waymo carries at least $5 million in liability insurance per incident as a condition of its Arizona operating permit. To exchange insurance information after a crash with a Waymo, the company directs drivers to waymo.com/notify or to contact the vehicle through the in-car speakers.

Because ARS 28-9702 designates the ADS as the legal driver, a negligence or product-liability claim runs against Waymo LLC, not against a human driver. If the ADS committed a moving violation that caused the crash, the violation is part of the liability record even if no citation was issued, because the documentation lives in police reports and NHTSA SGO filings.

The two-year statute of limitations for personal injury in Arizona (ARS 12-542) applies. If a Waymo crash caused you injury, the clock starts on the date of the crash.
