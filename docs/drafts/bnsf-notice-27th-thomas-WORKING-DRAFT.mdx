Four months before Sonja Celius was struck at 27th Avenue, BNSF told the Federal Railroad Administration in writing that vehicles were entering the twin crossing at Thomas Road from a direction that did not have gates.

That sentence is what the public record says. It is not a legal conclusion. It is the documentary record BNSF Railway Company created when its own train struck an occupied auto at the Thomas Road grade crossing in west Phoenix on December 15, 2025. BNSF filed Form 6180.57 with the Federal Railroad Administration. The narrative field of that filing, written by BNSF, reads in part: **"THE AUTO CAME UP AGAINST THE DIRECTION OF TRAFFIC FLOW NOT HAVING GATES."** A passenger in the auto was injured.

One hundred and thirty-one days later, Sonja Celius was struck as a pedestrian at the twin grade crossing 50 feet south on 27th Avenue.

## What the Federal Railroad Administration Record Says

The crossings have inventory numbers. DOT 025430G is the 27th Avenue grade crossing. DOT 025617C is Thomas Road. Both sit on a single BNSF main, the Phoenix Subdivision, mileposts 189.580 to 189.615. They are approximately 50 feet apart.

For roughly five decades of FRA inventory history, both crossings carried passive crossbucks. The wooden X. No gates, no flashing lights, no bells. The combined Form 6180.57 accident record at both crossings runs to approximately 70 reportable incidents since the late 1970s.

In June 2024, the City of Phoenix Streets Transportation Department and BNSF completed a project at both crossings. The city's project page describes the scope: new curbs, gutters, sidewalks, ramps, pavement restoration, traffic signal upgrades, new cantilevers, and new railroad gate arms. The FRA inventory revised July 18, 2025 confirms two-quadrant gates at both crossings, cantilever-mounted LED flashing lights, mast-mounted flashing lights, and bells.

The same FRA inventory shows what the project did not include. No channelizing medians (the physical barriers that prevent vehicles or pedestrians from going around a lowered gate arm). No four-quadrant gates (the configuration that closes all four sides of a crossing simultaneously). No pedestrian-specific gates. No wayside horns. WHISTBAN equals zero at both crossings.

Between June 2024 and April 25, 2026, BNSF filed three Form 6180.57 accident reports at the Thomas Road crossing. Each report is a separate event. Each is signed by BNSF Railway Company.

### June 15, 2025, Auto Preceded Gates

At 4:55 PM on June 15, 2025, a BNSF train designated YPHX203115 was pulling on the single main track. It struck an auto at the Thomas Road grade crossing. The temperature was 110 degrees Fahrenheit. The visibility was Day. The warning equipment was operational, including the gates.

The BNSF narrative for that Form 6180.57 filing, field 54, reads in full:

> "YPHX203115 PULLING ON SINGLE MAIN TRACK STRUCK AN AUTO THAT PRECEDED GATES AT A HGX. DRIVER TOOK OFF. NO DERAILMENT. NO HAZMAT RELEASE. NO INJURIES. USER'S AGE UNKNOWN."

No injuries. The driver fled the scene. The estimated vehicle damage was approximately $1,000.

### August 7, 2025, Auto Preceded Gates

At 3:40 PM on August 7, 2025, a BNSF train designated YPHX203107 was pulling on the single main track. It struck an occupied auto at the Thomas Road crossing. The temperature was 115 degrees Fahrenheit. The driver was a 25-year-old woman traveling alone.

The BNSF narrative, field 54:

> "YPHX203107 PULLING ON SINGLE MAIN TRACK STRUCK AN OCCUPIED AUTO AT HGX EQUIPPED WITH GATES. AUTO PRECEDED GATES. NO DERAILMENT. NO HAZMAT RELEASE. NO INJURIES."

The same phrase appears in BNSF's own writing. "AUTO PRECEDED GATES." A vehicle entered the crossing before the gate arm had fully descended.

### December 15, 2025, Direction Not Having Gates

At 2:45 AM on December 15, 2025, a BNSF train designated QPHXCHI114 was traveling light power on the single main track. It struck an occupied auto at the Thomas Road crossing. The temperature was 55 degrees Fahrenheit. The visibility was Dark. The driver was a 28-year-old man with two passengers in the vehicle.

The BNSF narrative, field 54, reads in full:

> "QPHXCHI114 LIGHT POWER ON SINGLE MAIN TRACK STRUCK AN OCCUPIED AUTO AT A HGX EQUIPPED WITH GATES. THE AUTO CAME UP AGAINST THE DIRECTION OF TRAFFIC FLOW NOT HAVING GATES. NO DERAILMENT. NO HAZMAT RELEASE. 1 PASSENGER INJURY."

One passenger was injured.

## What BNSF Identified, In Its Own Words

The three filings document, in BNSF's own writing, two distinct geometries that fall outside the protection of a two-quadrant gate.

The first geometry is the gate-timing race. A two-quadrant gate begins descending when flashing lights have already started cycling. The interval between the first flash and the gate fully down is governed by 49 CFR Part 234 and varies with crossing characteristics. In that interval, a vehicle moving at speed can enter the crossing before the gate is positioned to block its path. The Federal Highway Administration's crash-modification literature documents this failure mode. The standard mitigation, as set in the Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices Part 8, is a channelizing median. A raised concrete or curbed barrier on the centerline of the roadway, extending back from the crossing, prevents drivers from going around lowered gates and creates a physical constraint that interrupts the timing race.

The Thomas Road crossing has no channelizing median per the July 2025 FRA inventory. BNSF wrote "AUTO PRECEDED GATES" twice in two months at the same crossing in summer 2025.

The second geometry is the directional gap. A two-quadrant gate places a gate arm across each direction of through traffic. At a crossing with side streets, alley access, parking-lot egress, or median geometry that allows vehicle approach from a direction perpendicular to the through-traffic axis, the gate arms do not block that perpendicular approach. The federal solution is a four-quadrant gate, which closes all four corners of the crossing simultaneously. Neither 27th Avenue nor Thomas Road has a four-quadrant configuration per the July 2025 FRA inventory.

BNSF wrote "THE AUTO CAME UP AGAINST THE DIRECTION OF TRAFFIC FLOW NOT HAVING GATES" in the December 15, 2025 filing. One passenger was injured in that incident.

<Callout type="warning" title="What the federal record points to">
The Federal Railroad Administration Quiet Zones rule, 49 CFR Part 222, treats channelizing medians and four-quadrant gates as Supplementary Safety Measures. The Federal Highway Administration's Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices Part 8 sets the engineering criteria. Where a corridor presents the failure modes BNSF documented at Thomas Road in 2025, the federal framework identifies the mitigations and the conditions under which they apply. The Thomas Road and 27th Avenue crossings do not currently include those mitigations.
</Callout>

## What Form 6180.57 Is and What It Is Not

Form 6180.57 is the Federal Railroad Administration's Highway-Rail Grade Crossing Accident or Incident Report. Railroads operating on the general rail system file it after any reportable accident at a grade crossing. The form captures date, time, location, weather, equipment status, narrative description, casualties, and damage. The records are public under the Federal Railroad Safety Act and are searchable through the FRA Office of Safety Analysis.

The form is not an admission. Federal law, 49 USC 20903, restricts the admissibility of Form 6180.57 reports in damages litigation: the report "shall not be admitted as evidence or used for any purpose in any suit or action for damages growing out of any matter mentioned in said report." The underlying facts (the date, the equipment status, the weather, the witness accounts) are admissible through other discovery. The form itself is not.

For journalism, Form 6180.57 is a public record of what railroads have written about their own incidents. The narratives in the three Thomas Road filings from 2025 are BNSF's words. Not paraphrased. ## The Sonja Celius Strike at 27th Avenue

On April 25, 2026, shortly after 9 AM, Sonja Celius was struck by a BNSF train at the 27th Avenue grade crossing. Phoenix police closed the intersection for hours. Per AZ Family reporting by Mikayla on May 7, 2026, she survived with serious injuries and her family said both of her legs were amputated. ## What Is Not in the Federal Record

Form 6180.57 captures reportable accidents. Train-pedestrian and train-vehicle contact resulting in death, injury, or reportable property damage. It does not capture true near-miss events: a vehicle on the tracks that clears before a train arrives, a pedestrian on the right-of-way who walks off, a 911 call from a citizen reporting a stalled car. Those records exist elsewhere.

AZ Law Now's editorial team has drafted a five-request public-records packet to the Phoenix Police Department, the Arizona Department of Transportation Rail Section, the City of Phoenix Streets Transportation Department, the Arizona Attorney General Consumer Protection Section, and the Federal Railroad Administration Office of Railroad Safety. The requests cover calls for service to the corridor, interagency correspondence, the engineering basis-of-design document for the June 2024 retrofit, consumer-complaint files, and FRA inspector defect reports. The packet is at `data/outreach/records-requests/bnsf-notice-27th-thomas-records-packet.md`.

The requests are public-records requests under ARS 39-121 and 5 USC 552. They are not litigation discovery. Returns are expected within 10 to 30 business days under each statute.

## What the Public Record Suggests Should Come Next

The City of Phoenix Capital Improvement Program contains the funding line item that produced the June 2024 retrofit. The engineering basis-of-design memo for that project will identify why the project scope included two-quadrant gates rather than four-quadrant gates or channelizing medians, and whether the city or BNSF considered the additional supplementary safety measures. That memo, when produced under ARS 39-121, will be material to the public record on what was known and when.

The FHWA Section 130 program is the federal funding source for state and local rail-crossing safety projects. Section 130 funds typically require a benefit-cost analysis and prioritize crossings with the highest expected casualty reduction. Whether the June 2024 retrofit was Section 130-funded, whether the project ranked supplementary safety measures, and what the benefit-cost analysis showed are documentable through ADOT Rail Section records.

Other Arizona corridors with documented two-quadrant gate retrofit at twin adjacent crossings would offer comparison. The structural failure modes BNSF wrote down at Thomas Road in 2025 are not Phoenix-specific. They are the documented limitations of the two-quadrant gate configuration at any urban arterial crossing with comparable geometry. A statewide survey of comparable retrofits, comparable post-retrofit incident rates, and comparable supplementary-safety-measure investment is on the editorial queue.
