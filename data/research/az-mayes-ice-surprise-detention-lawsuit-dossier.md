## 3. The hazardous-chemical site across the street

This is the hook. The facts are unusually clean.

**Company (HIGH):** Rinchem Company, LLC. Chemical and gas warehouse operator.
**Address (HIGH):** 13290 W Sweetwater Avenue, Surprise, Arizona. Directly across the street from the proposed ICE facility.
Source: Rinchem corporate locations page (rinchem.com/locations/arizona-surprise-warehouse/); Northwest Valley Indivisible analysis citing federal records.

**Chemicals stored (HIGH):** Chlorine, hydrofluoric acid (hydrogen fluoride), and fluorine, plus other regulated substances supporting semiconductor industry supply chains.
Source: AG press release; Northwest Valley Indivisible analysis citing federal Tier II and EPA RMP filings.

**EPA Risk Management Plan filing (HIGH):** Rinchem's most recent RMP was filed 2026-01-01, three weeks before the federal warehouse purchase. The RMP does not contemplate the presence of a 1,500-bed locked detention facility next door.
Source: AG press release, quoted directly: the Rinchem RMP "does not consider the potential hazards incurred by the presence of a mass detention facility next door, nor does it contemplate risk mitigation measures responding to the same."

**Worst-case hazard zone (MEDIUM):** Worst-case release scenarios for these substances can require evacuation perimeters of one-half to one mile. Detainees inside a locked federal facility cannot self-evacuate.
Source: Phoenix New Times reporting; corroborates AG complaint summary. The exact RMP-listed worst-case distance was not pulled from the EPA eRMP database directly within the time budget. FLAGGED. Recommended retrieval: EPA eRMP public reading room, facility ID search by address.

**Tier II report (MEDIUM):** Rinchem's Tier II hazardous-chemical inventory is publicly available through Arizona Department of Environmental Quality (ADEQ). Specific quantities listed were not retrieved within the time budget. FLAGGED.

## 4. NEPA and environmental review framework

**National Environmental Policy Act (HIGH):** 42 U.S.C. 4321 et seq. Requires federal agencies to evaluate environmental impacts of major federal actions before commitment of resources. Detention facility construction or major repurpose using federal contracts is plainly a major federal action under traditional NEPA case law.

**DHS NEPA categorical exclusion framework (MEDIUM):** DHS Directive 023-01 and Instruction Manual 023-01-001-01 govern when DHS can claim a categorical exclusion. Categorical exclusions for facility purchase or major renovation are narrow and do not extend to detention operations near acutely hazardous chemical storage. The AG complaint argues no valid CatEx applies and no Environmental Assessment or Environmental Impact Statement was prepared or published.
Source: Federal documents as cited by AG press release. Direct retrieval of DHS Instruction Manual was not performed within the time budget.

**Comparable NEPA challenges 2025-2026 (HIGH):**
- *Maryland v. DHS/ICE* (D. Md., filed 2026-03-19). AG Anthony Brown alleged NEPA and APA violations on a Williamsport warehouse for a $102 million detention facility. Court issued a Temporary Restraining Order on 2026-03-11 and extended limits through 2026-04-16.
- *New Jersey v. DHS/ICE* (D.N.J., filed 2026-03-20). AG and Roxbury Township sought a preliminary injunction on a 1,500-bed warehouse conversion, asserting NEPA violations and zoning incompatibility.
- *Michigan v. DHS/ICE* (D. Mich., filed 2026-03-24). AG Dana Nessel and the City of Romulus alleged DHS failed to assess environmental impacts and failed to provide public notice or consult state and local officials.
- *Friends of the Everglades, Inc. v. Secretary of DHS* (11th Cir., decided 2026-04-21). The Eleventh Circuit vacated a preliminary injunction in the Florida "Alligator Alcatraz" case, holding the project failed the post-2023 NEPA threshold of "more than minimal federal funding and control" because the state had built and was operating the facility itself. This is the key adverse precedent. The Surprise case is materially different because the warehouse purchase, the renovation contracts, and the operator contracts all flow through DHS.
Source: stateimpactcenter.org, Center for Biological Diversity press release, casemine.com NEPA analysis.

## 5. AG authority and statutory standing

**Arizona statutory authority (HIGH):** Arizona Revised Statutes Title 41, Chapter 1, Article 4 (commencing at A.R.S. 41-191) establishes the Attorney General as the chief legal officer of the State and authorizes the AG to bring actions on behalf of the State to protect public interests including the public health, environment, and lawful application of federal law within Arizona.

**Federal standing (HIGH):** Arizona asserts standing under the Administrative Procedure Act, 5 U.S.C. 702 et seq., as a state injured by federal agency action that fails statutorily required process. Sister-state precedent in the 2026 Maryland, New Jersey, and Michigan cases supports state standing in NEPA challenges to ICE warehouse-conversion projects.

**Mayes' prior federal-government suits (MEDIUM):** AG Mayes has filed multiple actions against federal agencies during the Trump administration's second term, including challenges on federal funding clawbacks and immigration enforcement boundaries. A complete catalog was not pulled in the time budget. FLAGGED for follow-up if cited.
