**Voice:** Brendan Franks, AZ Law Now.

**Arizona has no codified, enforceable heat-illness standard as of compilation date. (HIGH.)** ADOSH cites under the General Duty Clause, federal OSH Act Section 5(a)(1). The April 9, 2026 ICA-approved Heat Guidelines from the Workplace Heat Safety Task Force are guidance, not rulemaking.

Source: NPR, https://www.npr.org/2024/04/12/1244316874/florida-blocks-heat-protections-for-workers-right-before-summer (covers both Texas and Florida); Construction Dive, https://www.constructiondive.com/news/heat-laws-construction-water-breaks-safety/716883/

**Florida comparison. (HIGH.)** Florida HB 433 (2024) prevents cities and counties from creating heat protections. No statewide standard.
Source: NPR, link above; Scientific American, https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/florida-considers-banning-local-heat-protections-for-workers/

**Phoenix municipal heat ordinance. (HIGH.)** Phoenix City Council passed an outdoor-worker heat protection ordinance covering city contractors. Date and full text need pull from phoenix.gov before publication. FLAG on date.

## 6. Statutory frame

**ARS Title 23, Chapter 6: Workers Compensation. (HIGH.)** https://www.azleg.gov/arsDetail/?title=23

**ARS 23-901 et seq. (HIGH.)** General workers compensation framework. Arizona is a no-fault, exclusive-remedy state for covered injuries. Common-law tort suit against the employer is barred for compensable workplace injury except in narrow exceptions.

**ARS 23-1041. (HIGH.)** Computation of average monthly wage. Temporary total disability is paid at 66 and two-thirds of average monthly wage, plus statutory dependent allowance. The Industrial Commission adjusts the maximum annually for the Arizona mean wage, capped at 5 percent year-over-year.
Source: https://www.azleg.gov/ars/23/01041.htm; corroborated at https://www.azhurtonthejob.com/workers-compensation/occupational-illnesses-arizona-workers/

**ARS 23-1043 through 23-1043.04. (HIGH.)** Occupational disease, including respiratory and repetitive use. The statute covers any disease that is "an expected consequence in a particular industry" and that may occur as a result of exposure over an indefinite period. **The structural problem for heat-illness claims:** heat injury (heat stroke, heat exhaustion) is more typically processed as an "injury by accident" under ARS 23-1021, not as an occupational disease. Counsel on either route requires the claimant to prove (1) work-causation, (2) absence of substantial outside-work exposure to the same hazard, and (3) the precipitating event. The Wisniewski firm summarizes this as the "connection between the illness and work activities, and the origin must be associated with a work-related risk." That causation requirement is the carrier denial lever.
Source: https://www.azleg.gov/ars/23/01043.htm and surrounding sections.

**ARS 23-1061. (HIGH.)** Notice of accident; form of notice; claim for compensation; reopening; payment of compensation; notification of injury. https://www.azleg.gov/ars/23/01061.htm
* ARS 23-1061(F): self-insured employers must "promptly report" any denial of a claim, with industry-standard 21-day timeframe.
* ARS 23-1061(M): self-insured employer must deny a claim within 21 days or compensate the employee. Applies where the employee has missed more than seven days of work.
Source: Schmidt, Sethi & Akmajian summary, https://www.azinjurylaw.com/workers-compensation-unfair-claims-practices

**ARS 23-1067 (HIGH on existence, MEDIUM on application).** Scheduled-injury commutation to lump sums; recovery determined by ability to return to suitable employment at pre-injury earning capacity. The proposal cites 23-1067 as the carrier-penalty section. **Correction: the carrier-penalty / unfair-claim-practice statute is ARS 23-930, not 23-1067.** See immediately below.

**ARS 23-930. Unfair claim processing practices; bad faith; civil penalties. (HIGH.)** This is the operative carrier-penalty section. https://www.azleg.gov/ars/23/00930.htm
* If ICA finds unfair claim processing or bad faith, it shall award the claimant a benefit penalty of 25 percent of the benefit amount or $500, whichever is more, on top of benefits owed.
* If ICA finds an employer, self-insured, insurance carrier, or claim processing representative has a "history or pattern of repeated unfair claim processing practices or bad faith," ICA may impose a civil penalty up to $1,000 per violation.
* The "unreasonable" denial standard: an unfair claim is one where the carrier "unreasonably issues a notice of claim status without adequate supporting information in its possession or available to it," fails to acknowledge and act reasonably on communications, advises the employee not to consult an attorney, or communicates directly with a represented employee for an improper purpose.

**ARS 39-121 et seq. (HIGH.)** Arizona Public Records Law. The vehicle for any newsroom request to ICA Claims Division for heat-illness claim cohort data, denial counts, and carrier-by-carrier breakdowns.

**ARS 12-611 et seq. (HIGH on existence, MEDIUM on heat-claim application.)** Wrongful death. Wrongful-death suits against employers are barred by the workers comp exclusive-remedy bar except where statutory exceptions apply (intentional tort, third-party tortfeasor, dual-capacity). Heat-death civil suits in Arizona typically run against third parties, not the direct employer. FLAG for any case-specific reference.

**ARS 23-1061 21-day denial clock vs. real carrier behavior. (FLAG, structural.)** The 21-day clock applies cleanly only where the worker missed more than seven days. For workers who returned to work or died before that threshold (heat deaths often occur on the day of exposure), the 21-day discipline does not bind the carrier in the same way. This is one structural reason heat-illness denials are easier than back-injury denials.

## 10. TOP 5 HIGH-CONFIDENCE FACTS for the lede

1. **Arizona has no codified, enforceable heat-illness workplace standard. ADOSH's State Emphasis Program (July 17, 2023) is guidance only. The ICA Heat Guidelines approved April 9, 2026 are guidance only. ADOSH still cites under the federal General Duty Clause, which requires the agency to prove heat is a recognized hazard at the cited site, that the employer failed to implement feasible controls, and that those controls would materially reduce risk. (HIGH, multi-source.)**

2. **California has both an outdoor heat standard (since 2005) and an indoor heat standard (effective July 23, 2024), and processes about 1,000 occupational heat-illness workers comp claims per year. Arizona has neither standard codified and does not publish a heat-illness claim count. (HIGH, Cal/OSHA Standards Board record.)**

3. **The Arizona workers comp framework gives carriers a 21-day denial clock under ARS 23-1061(M), but only where the worker missed more than seven days. Heat deaths often occur on the day of exposure. The structural 21-day discipline does not bind the carrier in those cases the same way it does for back-injury claims. (HIGH, ARS 23-1061; Schmidt, Sethi & Akmajian summary.)**

4. **The carrier-penalty statute the proposal cites as ARS 23-1067 is actually ARS 23-930. ARS 23-930 awards the claimant a 25 percent benefit penalty or $500, whichever is more, on a finding of unreasonable denial or bad faith, and authorizes ICA to impose civil penalties up to $1,000 per violation for a pattern of unfair claim processing. (HIGH, ARS 23-930 statute text.)**

5. **Maricopa County recorded 602 confirmed heat-related deaths in 2024. The county does not publish an occupational subset of that number. The Korman case settlement on April 15, 2026 reset the utility-disconnection rules. There is no parallel reset on the workers comp side. (HIGH, MCDPH 2024 final report; AG release for the Korman context.)**
