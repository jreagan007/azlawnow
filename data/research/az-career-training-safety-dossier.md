## 1. Federal funding pressure (Title IV schedule, 90/10, R2T4)

Proprietary (for-profit) career schools that participate in federal student aid operate under three structural pressures that shape enrollment, billing, and withdrawal practices. This context matters because financial incentives tied to aid drawdown can push a school to keep students enrolled past the point of reasonable attendance.

### R2T4 (Return of Title IV Funds), the 60% rule

After a student passes the 60% point of a payment period or period of enrollment, the school has earned 100% of the Title IV funds scheduled for that student. Before that point, earned aid is calculated pro rata.

> "After the 60% point in the payment period or period of enrollment, a student has earned 100% of the Title IV funds."

Cite: 34 CFR 668.22; Federal Student Aid Handbook 2025-2026, Volume 5, Chapter 1. https://fsapartners.ed.gov/knowledge-center/fsa-handbook/2025-2026/vol5/ch1-general-requirements-withdrawals-and-return-title-iv-funds

Pro rata formula: days or clock hours attended divided by total scheduled days/hours in the period. Cite: 34 CFR 668.22(e).

### Clock-hour program mechanics

Most massage, cosmetology, and short-credential vocational programs are clock-hour programs. They must measure both instructional hours AND weeks of instructional time completed. A student who does not complete all scheduled clock hours and weeks in a payment period triggers R2T4.

Cite: 34 CFR 668.22(a)(2)(i).

### Timing of withdrawal determination

For modular programs, a student who ceases attendance and isn't scheduled to return to another course within 45 days of the end of a module must be treated as withdrawn. Non-term and subscription programs use a 60-day threshold.

Cite: 34 CFR 668.22(a)(2)(ii).

Institution must complete the R2T4 calculation within 30 days of the date of determination and return funds to the Department within 45 days.

### New R2T4 regs effective July 1, 2026

The Department of Education published final rules January 3, 2025. Effective July 1, 2026. https://fsapartners.ed.gov/knowledge-center/library/electronic-announcements/2026-03-27/implementation-return-title-iv-funds-r2t4-regulations-effective-july-1-2026

### 90/10 rule

A proprietary institution can't derive more than 90% of tuition and fee revenue from federal education assistance funds in any fiscal year. At least 10% must come from other sources. This creates a well-documented incentive to aggressively enroll cash-pay students or students with non-federal loans to balance the ratio, and to count veteran education benefits within or outside the federal bucket depending on policy year.

Cite: Higher Education Act Section 487(a)(24); 34 CFR 668.28.

### Why this matters to personal-injury and consumer-protection counsel

The R2T4 60% threshold creates a financial waterline. A school that can hold a student past the 60% mark keeps all the aid. Combined with clock-hour accounting (every missed hour counts), these rules make attendance pressure a structural feature. When schools push students into clinical practicum hours to meet scheduled completion, the supervision model is where risk concentrates.

## 4. National precedents (Corinthian, ITT, Borrower Defense trend)

Structural precedents that color jury and regulator attitudes toward for-profit career schools. Primary-source summaries, not speculation.

### Corinthian Colleges (2015 collapse, 2022 discharge)

Largest for-profit education collapse in US history. On June 1, 2022, the Department of Education announced full discharge of all remaining federal student loans owed by approximately 560,000 Corinthian borrowers, totaling roughly $5.8 billion. US Department of Education press release: https://www.ed.gov/news/press-releases/education-department-approves-58-billion-group-discharge-fully-cancel-loans-560000-borrowers-who-attended-corinthian-colleges

Basis: findings that Corinthian misrepresented job placement rates, career services, and transferability of credits across its Everest, Heald, and WyoTech brands.

### ITT Technical Institute (2016 closure)

ITT closed all 130+ US campuses September 2016 after Department of Education restrictions. In 2022 the Department announced $3.9 billion in full discharges for approximately 208,000 former ITT students. https://www.ed.gov/news/press-releases/department-education-announces-approval-39-billion-group-discharge-208000-borrowers-who-attended-itt-technical-institute

Findings referenced misrepresentations about job placement, credit transferability, program cost, and partnerships with employers.

### Borrower Defense to Repayment, sustained trend

34 CFR 685.206 and related regs allow federal loan discharge where a school substantially misrepresented program outcomes, used aggressive or misleading recruiting, or breached contract. Department has processed hundreds of thousands of claims across dozens of institutions 2021-2025. Current rule set: https://studentaid.gov/manage-loans/forgiveness-cancellation/borrower-defense

### Why this matters for AZ

These federal discharges signal regulator and judicial receptivity to claims of structural recruiting and supervision failure at proprietary schools. They don't establish Arizona-school liability, but they shift the baseline plausibility when an AZ plaintiff pleads similar facts.

## 5. Arizona tort law: negligent hiring / supervision / retention

### Doctrinal basis

Arizona recognizes direct-negligence theories against employers for negligent hiring, negligent supervision, and negligent retention of employees who injure third parties during the scope of that employment relationship.

Leading case: **Pruitt v. Pavelin**, 141 Ariz. 195, 685 P.2d 1347 (App. 1984). Established that an employer owes a duty of reasonable care in selecting employees, particularly where the employee will have contact with members of the public.

Expanded: **Mulhern v. City of Scottsdale**, 165 Ariz. 395, 799 P.2d 15 (App. 1990).

Restatement analogue: Restatement (Second) of Agency § 213 and Restatement (Second) of Torts § 317.

### Elements (plaintiff must prove)

1. The employer owed a duty to the plaintiff to exercise reasonable care in hiring, supervising, or retaining the employee.
2. The employer breached that duty.
3. The employee was unfit or a threat in a way the employer knew or should have known.
4. The unfitness was the proximate cause of plaintiff's injury.
5. Damages.

### Application to career-school fact patterns

Where a career-school clinical practicum puts a member of the public into physical contact with a student therapist, massage practitioner, medical assistant trainee, or cosmetology student under school supervision, the school holds the "employer" role over the supervising instructor AND the entity directing the student's conduct. Theories available:

- Negligent supervision of instructor
- Negligent supervision of student during supervised practicum
- Negligent training (schools as defendants)
- Negligent retention if prior complaints on instructor ignored

Companion theories: breach of fiduciary duty where the school holds itself out as a consumer-protection gatekeeper; consumer fraud under A.R.S. 44-1522 where marketing misstated supervision ratios, placement rates, or licensure eligibility.

### Damages cap and liability exposure

Arizona has no state-law cap on personal-injury damages in tort (Ariz. Const. art. 2 § 31 bars damage caps in death and injury actions). Punitive damages available on clear-and-convincing showing of evil mind, spite, or malice.

## 6. Title IX + Clery applicability gap

### Title IX

Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972 (20 USC § 1681) applies to any education program or activity receiving federal financial assistance. That covers Title-IV-participating proprietary schools. https://www2.ed.gov/about/offices/list/ocr/docs/tix_dis.html

### Clery Act

The Jeanne Clery Disclosure of Campus Security Policy and Campus Crime Statistics Act (20 USC § 1092(f)) requires institutions that participate in Title IV programs to publish an Annual Security Report, maintain a daily crime log, and issue timely warnings of Clery-geography crimes.

### The gap, where coverage thins

1. **Short clock-hour schools** often publish minimal ASRs. Compliance is uneven and enforcement-driven, not routine.
2. **Off-site clinical practicum locations.** A school-operated massage clinic in leased retail space, a student-run salon at a mall, an externship site at a third-party clinic. These may or may not fall inside "Clery geography" depending on the school's written control agreement.
3. **Title IX's supervisory reach over students-as-providers** is less developed than its reach over students-as-victims. If a public-facing practicum client is injured by a student, Title IX alone isn't the obvious vehicle. State tort and A.R.S. 44-1522 claims carry more weight.
4. **Non-Title-IV schools** (schools that elect not to take federal aid) are exempt from Clery and Title IX. Arizona licenses these through PPSE without federal coverage.

### Investigative angle

Any AZ career school that markets itself to the public as a low-cost clinic "staffed by students under instructor supervision" should be checked for:
- Title IV participation status (confirms Clery and Title IX obligations)
- Current ASR publication on the school website
- PPSE license status
- Any BBB, AG Consumer Protection, or sector-board complaints
