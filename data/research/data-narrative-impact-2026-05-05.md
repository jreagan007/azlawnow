# Data Narrative Impact Analysis: AZ Law Now Embed-Bait Visuals

**Compiled:** 2026-05-05
**Purpose:** Rank candidate visual frames by gut-punch impact for journalist-embed pickup. Audience for the visual is a working journalist asking "would my readers stop scrolling?"
**Stories analyzed:**
1. AZ Workers Comp Heat-Claim Denials (`/investigations/arizona-workers-comp-heat-denials/`)
2. AZ Nursing Homes Billing Audit + Care Audit (`/investigations/arizona-nursing-homes-billing-fraud-and-abuse/`)

___

## Operating frame

Three-second test. The reader looks at the visual once. What number, person, or contrast hits them in the chest before they read a single word of caption?

The current Workers Comp visual fails the three-second test because codification status is structural, not visceral. A reader who understands the difference between "codified standard" and "guidance" is already a policy person. To everyone else, the choropleth is a colored map. The 602 anchor sits in the panel, not the eye-line.

The fix is to lead with bodies, not buckets. Bodies = death counts, named workers, residents in low-rated buildings. Buckets = regulatory categories, statutory citations, codification typology.

Use buckets only after bodies have done the emotional work.

## STORY 1: AZ Workers Comp Heat-Claim Denials

### Frame A: The Standards Gap Map (current top pick)

**Single sentence:** "Four states have codified workplace heat standards. Arizona has guidance. Texas and Florida banned local rules."
**Anchor data point:** 4 states codified vs. AZ guidance + TX/FL preempted (regulatory typology).
**Visual structure:** US choropleth. Color-coded states. Side panel with 602 deaths + 6 named decedents + ARS 23-930 math.
**Why it underperforms:** The map is the eye-anchor, but the map doesn't carry the death toll. The 602 number lives in the panel. Choropleths don't move people. They explain. The current visual is magazine-quality, not stop-the-scroll quality. A journalist looking for an embed wants the headline IN the image, not next to it.
**Verification posture:** All HIGH. Cal/OSHA T8 CCR 3395 (since 2005), Oregon OAR 437,002,0156 (2022), WA WAC 296,307,097 (2008/2023), TX HB 2127 (2023), FL HB 433 (2024). 602 figure HIGH (MCDPH 2024 surveillance). Decedents HIGH (KJZZ 2026,04,06 named record).

___

### Frame B: The Death Curve (RANKED #2)

**Single sentence:** "Maricopa heat deaths nearly tripled from 2014 to 2024. Other states wrote rules. Arizona wrote guidance."
**Anchor data point:** A line chart climbing from ~2014 baseline to 602 in 2024, with the 2005 California codification marked as a vertical event line at the far left, 2008 Washington, 2022 Oregon, 2024 Cal/OSHA indoor, and a tiny Arizona "guidance only" marker at April 2026 on the right edge.
**Visual structure:** Line chart, full bleed. Y-axis is deaths. X-axis is years 2014 to 2024. Vertical drop-down marks for the codification dates of other states, labeled with state initials. The Arizona guidance marker is a thin gray line, the others are bold.
**Why it outperforms:** The eye lands on the climbing line first. The codification markers tell the comparative story without a legend. The reader gets "the line went up while other states fixed it" in one glance. This is the causal narrative the choropleth flattens. Bonus: it works at thumbnail size on social. The choropleth doesn't.
**Verification posture:** Climbing trend HIGH (MCDPH historical surveillance). Codification dates HIGH (cited above). One MEDIUM/FLAG dependency: the year-by-year Maricopa heat death series 2014 to 2024 is referenced as "first decrease since 2014" in the dossier (HIGH for the 2024 number, MEDIUM for each individual prior year). Pull MCDPH historic series before publication. If MCDPH only publishes annual snapshots, the curve still works on the published year-end totals.

### Frame C: The Six Names (RANKED #1)

**Single sentence:** "Six Arizona workers died from heat. Their employers, their dates, their jobs. Arizona's claim denial rate is unpublished."
**Anchor data point:** Six names. Six dates. Six job titles. One number underneath: "Heat-illness workers comp denial rate, Arizona: not published."
**Visual structure:** Full-bleed black panel. Six names in a serif type at 90pt, stacked. Under each name: job title, city, date of death. Bottom strip: a single line of small white text: "Arizona doesn't publish a heat-illness workers comp claim count. California processes about 1,000 per year." No chart. No map. No icons. Pure typography against black.
**Why it outperforms (decisively):** This is the only frame that forces the reader to grieve before they think. The choropleth asks the reader to compare states. The death curve asks the reader to interpret a trend. The six names ask nothing. They name. Journalists embed visuals that make their audience feel something they can't get from a chart. When the New York Times runs a Covid-anniversary front page of names, this is the principle. When the Vietnam Memorial works, this is the principle. The names aren't a chart, they're a reckoning. The "Arizona doesn't publish" line is the kicker, not the headline. The names are the headline. This is the embed visual that gets picked up by the AZ Republic, KJZZ, the Cronkite News, and re-shared organically.
**Verification posture:** All six names HIGH (KJZZ 2026,04,06 named on public record). Job titles HIGH. Dates HIGH. The structural absence of a published denial rate HIGH (ICA does not publish; primary source: dossier section 2). Family-permission flag: KJZZ named these workers because their families or employers had already named them publicly. Re-publishing names that families already authorized to KJZZ is not a new permission ask, but Brendan Franks should confirm KJZZ's posture on permission-by-attribution before AZ Law Now uses the names in a stand-alone visual context. Recommend a one-paragraph "How we named them" caption on the embed page.

### Frame D: The 21-Year Gap (RANKED #3)

**Single sentence:** "California codified its heat standard in 2005. Arizona issued guidance in 2026. 21 years passed. Workers died."
**Anchor data point:** 21-year gap, with annual heat death counts climbing along it.
**Visual structure:** Horizontal timeline, full bleed. Left anchor: 2005, "California codifies (T8 CCR 3395)." Right anchor: 2026, "Arizona issues guidance." Along the timeline, year-by-year tick marks for Maricopa heat deaths, growing from a small dot to a big circle at 602. Other-state codification events plotted along the way (WA 2008, OR 2022, Cal/OSHA indoor 2024).
**Why it outperforms:** The number "21 years" is the gut-punch. Most readers will not have framed Arizona's regulatory failure as a generational failure. This frame does. It's also the frame that travels best in policy-reform contexts (advocates and legislators love the "21 years" headline).
**Why it ranks behind C and B:** The horizontal scan is harder than the vertical climb of Frame B. Timelines force the eye to read left-to-right; charts let the eye absorb shape instantly. Slightly more cognitive load.
**Verification posture:** All codification dates HIGH. 21-year gap arithmetic HIGH. Year-by-year death totals same MEDIUM/FLAG as Frame B (pull MCDPH historic series).

### Frame E: The Black Box (RANKED #4)

**Single sentence:** "California publishes 1,000 heat-illness claims per year. Arizona publishes nothing. The number is hidden."
**Anchor data point:** Two boxes. California: "1,000/year." Arizona: "?"
**Visual structure:** Two equal-sized boxes side by side. CA box filled with "1,000." AZ box is a black void with a single question mark. Caption below: "Arizona's Industrial Commission does not publish a heat-illness workers comp claim count. We requested it under ARS 39-121."
**Why it outperforms parts of A:** Forces the reader to confront the absence. The void is its own data point. Strong frame for a follow-up "we filed the records request" story.
**Why it ranks lower:** Less emotional than C, less cinematic than B and D. Strongest as a secondary visual paired with C (names get the shock, void gets the policy ask).
**Verification posture:** California 1,000/year HIGH (Cal/OSHA Standards Board ISOR). Arizona non-publication HIGH (dossier section 2; ICA bulletins do not break out heat-illness subset).

### Frame F: The Penalty Math On Real Cases (RANKED #5)

**Single sentence:** "ARS 23-930 caps the bad-faith denial penalty at 25% or $500. The Korman heat-disconnect settlement was $7M."
**Anchor data point:** $500 vs. $7M (proportional bar comparison).
**Visual structure:** Two horizontal bars, drastically asymmetric. Top: "$500 (ARS 23-930 minimum bad-faith penalty)." Bottom: "$7,000,000 (APS Korman heat-disconnect AG settlement, 2026,04,15)." The visual contrast in bar length is the message.
**Why it underperforms:** Apples-to-oranges risk. The Korman settlement is a utility-disconnection consumer-fraud action (ARS 44-1522), not a workers comp bad-faith penalty. The bar comparison is technically correct but conceptually muddled. A journalist would push back on the framing, which is the kiss of death for embed pickup.
**Verification posture:** $500 floor HIGH (ARS 23-930). $7M Korman settlement HIGH (AG release 2026,04,15). The conceptual link between them is FLAGGED.

## STORY 2: AZ Nursing Homes Billing Audit + Care Audit

### Frame A: The 142/52 Facility Dot Map (current top pick, RANKED #3)

**Single sentence:** "142 nursing homes in Arizona. 52 carry a 1- or 2-star CMS rating."
**Anchor data point:** 52 of 142 (37%).
**Visual structure:** AZ map with all 142 facilities dotted. The 52 low-rated facilities colored sienna. Maricopa cluster, Pima cluster.
**Why it underperforms vs. higher-ranked frames:** The map shows distribution but doesn't show who is harmed. A dot is not a person. The cluster pattern is interesting to a planner, less so to a daughter wondering whether her mother's facility is on the list. Same problem as the choropleth in Story 1: structurally honest, emotionally muted.
**Verification posture:** 142 facilities MEDIUM (CMS Care Compare aggregate; pull live CSV before publication). 52 1-or-2-star MEDIUM (same source). FLAG: confirm count holds at publication week.

___

### Frame B: The Substantiation Gap Bar (RANKED #1)

**Single sentence:** "Arizona substantiates fewer than 1% of nursing-home abuse reports. The national rate is 29 to 33%."
**Anchor data point:** AZ <1% vs. national 29 to 33% (substantiation rate).
**Visual structure:** Two horizontal bars. National bar: a thick wide block, 30%. Arizona bar: a barely-visible sliver, <1%. No decoration. No icons. The visual contrast is the entire message. Caption: "Arizona Adult Protective Services received more than 69,000 communications in 2024. It substantiated fewer than 1%."
**Why it outperforms decisively:** This is the kind of bar chart that goes viral in policy circles. The visual is impossible to misread. The implication is impossible to escape: either Arizona's APS investigators are 30x more accurate than every other state's, or 30x more reports are getting buried. Neither answer is comfortable. Every elder-care advocacy org in the country will share this if it ships. AARP. Consumer Voice. Justice in Aging. The Long-Term Care Ombudsman networks. Embed value is enormous because the chart names a problem the reader didn't know existed in three seconds.
**Verification posture:** MEDIUM with FLAG. The dossier flags the substantiation-rate figure as MEDIUM (aggregated reporting on DES APS data). Pre-publication action required: pull the primary DES APS SFY 2024 Annual Report and confirm the <1% figure against the source document. If the figure holds against primary source, this is the strongest single visual across both stories. If it doesn't hold, the visual cannot ship and Frame C becomes the new #1.

### Frame C: Allegiant Healthcare Of Mesa Timeline (RANKED #2)

**Single sentence:** "33 violations since 2019. $8,400 fine in 2023 after a resident sustained injuries of unknown origin. Still operating."
**Anchor data point:** 33 violations / $8,400 fine / "still operating."
**Visual structure:** Calendar grid 2019 to 2025. Each violation plotted as a dot on its month. The 2023 abuse-related fine called out with a red marker and "$8,400" label. Bottom-right of the grid: a stamp-style "STILL OPERATING" mark.
**Why it outperforms A:** Specificity beats abstraction. One named facility, one named pattern, one named outcome. Journalists love the "still operating" tag because it's the implied call to action without saying it. Reader takeaway in 3 seconds: "33 violations and they're still open?" That's the embed.
**Why it ranks behind B:** Single-facility frames depend on the facility staying named. Frame B is structural and applies to every reader's grandmother. Frame C is specific and applies to one building's residents. Both are powerful; B reaches further.
**Verification posture:** 33 violations MEDIUM (CMS Care Compare via aggregator; pull current Form 2567 narratives). $8,400 fine MEDIUM (same). "Still operating" HIGH (current CMS-listed status). Pre-publication: pull the live Form 2567 narrative for Allegiant Healthcare of Mesa from medicare.gov/care-compare. The dossier flags this as a MEDIUM-to-HIGH conversion that needs one fetch before publication.

### Frame D: The Population Unit Chart (RANKED #4)

**Single sentence:** "Roughly 1 in 3 Arizonans living in a nursing home lives in a 1- or 2-star facility."
**Anchor data point:** ~14,000 people, with ~37% in low-rated buildings.
**Visual structure:** A unit chart of 14,000 dots (or 1,400 dots, each = 10 people). Dots colored by CMS star rating. The 1- and 2-star block colored sienna, occupies roughly 37% of the cloud. The visual makes the person count legible.
**Why it outperforms A:** Translates "52 of 142 facilities" into "5,000 of 14,000 people." The reader feels the population size, not the facility count. Person counts hit harder than facility counts.
**Why it ranks lower than B and C:** The 14,000 population figure is MEDIUM in the dossier (industry-standard ALTCS estimate; not pulled from primary AHCCCS dashboard). And the unit chart, while striking, requires more visual density and time to read than the bar comparison in Frame B.
**Verification posture:** 14,000 figure MEDIUM (industry estimate; must pull from AHCCCS ALTCS enrollment dashboard before publication). 1-or-2-star share MEDIUM (CMS Care Compare). Strongest if both numbers are confirmed against primary AHCCCS and CMS files.

## The single most underused data point across both stories

**"Arizona doesn't publish the number."**

Both stories are, structurally, the same story: a state agency that owns the data refuses (or fails) to surface it. Workers Comp: ICA does not publish heat-illness claim counts or denial rates. Nursing Homes: AHCCCS does not publish an aggregate billing-fraud citation count, and APS does not surface its substantiation-rate gap.

This is a second-order narrative finding that neither current visual frames around. The Workers Comp choropleth shows the regulatory typology. The Nursing Homes dot map shows facility distribution. Neither shows the absence of public data, which is the through-line of the entire AZ Law Now editorial mission.

Recommendation: every embed visual AZ Law Now ships should carry a small but readable caption identifying the public records request that should exist. "We filed [N] under ARS 39-121. Here's what the agency wouldn't surface." This builds AZ Law Now as the publication that names the gap, not just the publication that reports the harm. Over six months of consistent publication, that posture becomes brand identity.

___

## Verdict on the current Workers Comp visual

**Close-but-not-quite. The right concept (data-led, magazine-quality, ships with verified anchors), the wrong centerpiece (choropleth doesn't carry the emotional weight the story requires).**

The current visual is the visual a journalist would build for a wonk policy publication (Stateline, Route Fifty, Pew Trusts research brief). The visual AZ Law Now needs is one tier more visceral, because the audience pickup target is broader (mainstream daily papers + advocacy + social).

**Recommended action:**
1. Keep the choropleth as the in-article supporting graphic. It carries the comparative state data well and rewards the policy-literate reader.
2. Build Frame C (The Six Names) as the new lead embed. Black panel, serif type, six names. Ship it as the journalist-pitch primary visual. Use the choropleth as the "if you also want a map" backup.
3. Build Frame B (The Death Curve) as the second pitch visual. Climbing line + state codification markers. This is the "show me the trend" embed for reporters who want a chart.

The goal is not one perfect visual. The goal is a primary + backup pair per story, so the journalist has options and AZ Law Now ships as a publication with editorial range, not just one good chart.
